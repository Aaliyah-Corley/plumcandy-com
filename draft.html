Preface

 

Is it really a betrayal if you should have seen it coming? You can’t think about that now.

Darkness presses in, heavy, sapping, but not the kind of dark to hide in. You don’t see anyone, but are you alone? No.

Your body knows before the mind. Hair stands up; a million nerve extensions seek out danger. The crawling sensation up your spine snaps a whip: glance behind.

Freeze. Hold that noisy breath. Listen.

You hear nothing. Nothing out of the ordinary. Not yet. How can it be so quiet and loud at the same time? It’s the heightened sense that both the hunted and the hunter share, that they’ve had to share since before time had a name. Pure survival.

Before the fall it was all so simple.

Now? Some people have their heart ripped out, others have their heart ripped out.

That’s the tightening in your chest, the sinking sensation. The dreaded void.

Will you run? Will you fight? Will you decide before it’s too late?

All an illusion. There’s no real choice.

It only feels like time is standing still. It is not.

You’re gonna run. Go! GO!

…

All quiet now.

Chapter 1

Noelan Elyrite keeps his eyes fixed on the winding path ahead, this time the dirty road through Pine Ridge. Heading south, the morning sun warms his face. Noelan’s black hair doesn’t worry about a strong breeze from an open window, and neither does he.

In the passenger seat, Lawson Wright works in Noelan’s shadow. His finger traces a paper map, “Should be just over those mountains. Pine Ridge. Quaint little town, if you believe the reports.”

Noelan smirked, a casual one-handed grip on the steering wheel. “Small town, small problems? That it? Let’s see how long that keeps up. Guess we could use a plain Jane case for once.”

“This straight shot looks a little dodgy,” Lawson tapped the map. “The highway will take a wee bit longer, roundabout as it is, but it’ll get us there without any headaches. Bear to the right at the fork up here.”

Noelan nodded, grinned, and kept going straight.

The car roared upward, climbing, revving, straining against that relentless slope, kept pushing, onward! it toiled and battled against cruel twists and dead-drop turns, onward!

A sharp bend reshaped the view. Sunlight became a blade that stabbed through the windshield and punished Noelan’s eyes. He squinted. Motes of fractured color. His peripheral, blind. He had to focus or they’d fly off a cliff. Lawson was saying something, but it faded to nothing while Noelan focused round the dangerous turn.

After the crest, the descent began. The shadow of the ridge brought clarity. He started to see. There were trees, orange, yellow, red and evergreen pines and that small, insignificant-looking town. Those roads and cabins clung on to the east valley ridge. Continuing down, his vision cleared more.

There was a lake, too.

As the car whizzed by, Noelan read aloud, very loud, a roadside sign, varnished and carved so lovingly it could’ve hung in a church: Pine Ridge, Population 5,134.

“There,” Lawson pointed, “looks like a filling station. We should top up before heading into town.”

Noelan rolled in beside a gas pump while the station’s bell rattled out like old bones. The clanging brought out a scrawny attendant with a grease-smudged forehead… couldn’t have been older than twenty.

“I’ll handle this.” Noelan stepped out of the car. “Need to stretch my legs anyway.”

The attendant nodded a greeting. “Good afternoon, sir. Fill ’er up?”

The sign above the pump read ‘REGULAR 27¢/GAL’

Noelan replied with a swagger, “Thanks kid. I’ll square up inside.”

Gasoline pumping, price ticker spinning, Noelan made his way into the store. Above the door, another bell clanged.

Inside: chrome counter, AM radio crackling through doo-wop, maps on a rack. Newly printed magazines vied for attention over a mess of locally made knickknacks and penny candies.

On the wall behind the counter, a framed newspaper clip headlined: ‘A PIECE OF FISHSTORY’ with a picture of a man smiling while holding a particularly large trout. The caption read: ‘Mayor Coolidge congratulates Ted Larson on record catch!’ But someone had circled certain letters in red pen, then hastily attempted to erase the marks.

Noelan noticed that the photographed local fishing legend was the shopkeeper behind the counter, “Howdy, stranger!”, who’d just greeted him.

“Afternoon, Ted,” Noelan responded. “What do I owe you for the gas?”

The shopkeeper answered as the store’s bell jingled again.

A man stumbled in. The bandanna was red and paisley. Paisley! Like some dime-novel outlaw’s idea of a menacing mask. His clothes hung loose on his frame, perhaps “borrowed” from someone larger and too slow to catch him.

His hand, though shaky, did brandish a revolver.

“Hands up! Money in the bag, now!” the man yelled.

The shopkeeper reached for the ceiling, then asked, “What bag? You got one for me or…”

Noelan’s chin flicked upward sharp as a switchblade. Not worried, but amused. Amused by the robber’s dime-novel demeanor and kid-cowboy mask.

He swung the gun towards Noelan. “I said hands up!”

Swift but relaxed, Noelan snatched the culprit’s wrist and threw him on the ground. The gun clattered pathetically to the floor as the robber yelped in pain, “Aieee, ow, ow, ow! Gyaaah! Mercy! Uncle!”

Before the man knew what happened, Noelan had him back on his feet, face-down on the counter, arm twisted behind his back. The whole kerfuffle lasted barely enough time to be a kerf.

“Got any rope?” Noelan asked the gawking shopkeeper.

“What?!? You gonna hang him?”

“No. To tie him up. What kind of town is this?”

“Small town. No rope. Got duct tape, though.” The shopkeeper reached under the counter and tossed a roll to Noelan.

Noelan flipped the punk face-down across the counter, yanked his wrists together, and wrapped them tight with the silver tape (three quick loops, rip, done), then snagged the revolver off the floor, made sure it wasn’t cocked, and slipped it in his waistband.

“Now then,” Noelan turned to the shopkeeper, his tone conversational, “how much for the gas, again?” He glanced at the bound man, “let’s add a bottle of pop for our unexpected guest.”

“Duct tape, too?” grinned the shopkeeper.

Noelan chuckled and paid the tab, then hauled out the trussed-up robber with the Coke bottle dangling off two fingers like some cheap brown trophy.

As Noelan opened the back door of the car and ushered the bound man inside, Lawson inquired, “Geez. Was that on sale?”

“Small town. Already making new friends,” Noelan replied.

He slid into the driver’s seat, started the engine, and moseyed away from the gas station, the crime scene, and two gobsmacked yokels.

Back on the winding road, Lawson turned in his seat to eye their straggly captive, then back to his partner. “Noelan,” he said slowly, “We are here about the disappearances. Not to play beat cops.”

Noelan didn’t reply.

Lawson sighed and looked ahead. “We are headed to see Chief Travis anyway. I guess we could drop off some trash while we’re there.”

“So, care to explain yourself, friend?” Noelan asked, same tone as ordering the Coke.

The bound man shuffled uncomfortably in his seat. “First off, my name’s not ‘friend,’ it’s Bennet,” he grumbled. Then, his voice dropping to a near-whisper, he added, “And I did it because I needed dough for the protection fee...”

Lawson wasn't buying it. “Protection fee? What are you yapping about?”

Bennet looked out the window, his face reflective. “You’re new in town, ain’t ya? Don’t know how things work here in Pine Ridge.”

Noelan side-eyed Lawson. This could be exactly the kind of information they had come to Pine Ridge to uncover.

“Is it story time already?” Noelan asked.

Bennet took a deep breath. “About two months ago the goons got more serious about the occasional light envelope. Before that it was more subtle. A little short just meant eatin’ a knuckle sandwich. But then, everyone got put on this strict weekly drop. And if you don’t pay...”

He trailed off, his voice choked with emotion.

“What happens if you can’t pay?” Noelan pressed.

“Someone you love gets grabbed,” Bennet said, voice low and jagged. “Wife, kid, mom… they’re gone ‘til you pay up. And if you can’t get square? That special someone gets offed right in front of ’ya.”

Silence slammed into the car. Noelan just reached up and adjusted the rearview mirror, a tiny click that framed Bennet’s miserable face in the glass. Then he settled back, one hand resting on the wheel like a stone. The engine hummed steady with the patience of a guillotine.

“That’s why I stuck up the joint,” Bennet finally coughed up. “My fiancée... they said she looked too pretty for the gal of a broke mug like me. Promised to make the world right if I didn’t pony up by tonight. I couldn’t let that happen. I just couldn’t!”

“Hmm,” was all Lawson Wright replied, a look of skepticism on his face.

Noelan pulled the car into the long driveway when he found the sign and drove on until he saw it.

The Pine Ridge Inn: two stories of raw pine stacked like a lumber baron’s after-hours binge.

“I’ll wait here with our guest,” Lawson said. “You go book us a room.”

Glaring coldly at Bennet in the backseat, Noelan said, “Alright. Stay put. Got it, knucklehead?”

Noelan stepped out. The crunch of leaves underfoot punctuated an eerie stillness that blanketed Pine Ridge. Rolling up, the Inn’s silhouette had loomed frontier-old, but up close, with the logs still bleeding sap, the sniff said otherwise.

Inside felt warm and cozy: a fire going, wood polish, something floral like lilac... maybe. Behind a counter sat a man in his mid-forties, his friendly face creased with laugh lines that spoke of a life well-lived. Despite his genial appearance, Noelan noted the man’s broad shoulders and the hint of muscle beneath his rolled-up sleeves.

“Welcome to Pine Ridge Inn.” His voice was warm. His stare was cold. And the chair? It creaked and groaned as the Innkeeper rose. “What brings you to our neck of the woods?”

Noelan leaned casually against the counter. “Exploring. Thought I’d get off the main road, and experience some of this countryside.”

“Always good to broaden your horizons. You never know what nuggets of wisdom you might find... or what purpose you might fulfill.”

Noelan sighed slightly. “What are you going on about?”

The Innkeeper chuckled and waved a hand dismissively. “Oh, don’t mind me. Small town. Big imagination. How long will you be staying?”

“Six nights, if you have the room. Two beds, please.”

While the Innkeeper checked the ledger, Noelan took a gander around. Photos lined the walls, smiling families, local landmarks, a younger version of the Innkeeper with his arm around a pretty woman.

His gaze bounced from the photos to a glass jar on the counter, filled with small, individually wrapped, sweet, sweet candies where it settled, curiosity kindled.

The Innkeeper noticed Noelan’s interest and nodded. “Ah, you’ve spotted our little specialty.” He tapped the jar. “Plum candies; made right here in Pine Ridge. Go ahead, take as many as you like.”

Noelan reached into the jar and pulled out a candy. He unwrapped and popped it into his mouth, enjoying the burst of flavor. “Sweet,” he remarked, almost to himself.

Noelan smiled, letting the candy dissolve slowly as he looked around the cozy inn. He rolled it across his tongue. Then he really bit into it, crunching it into a mess of sharp shards in his mouth. “Though it’s got quite the bitter kick at the end.”

The Innkeeper chuckled. “Some say they’re the taste of the town. Keeps folks coming back.”

Noelan kept his face stoic, but the taste was absurdly delicious. Harsh, but strangely satisfying.

“You’re in luck,” the Innkeeper said, sliding a key across the counter. “Room 7, top of the stairs. Best view in the house. Enjoy your stay, Mr...?”

“Noelan. Just Noelan.”

“Enjoy your stay, Just Noelan,” the Innkeeper said, “and if you need anything, like advice, directions, or a good cup of joe, you know where to find me.”

Noelan took a fistful of candies, nodded, and strode out. Just as he left the inn, a soft weight on his foot gave him pause. Glancing down, he found himself under the scrutiny of a small cat. Its fluffy white and light gray fur gleamed in the sunlight.

Gently, Noelan scooped up the little feline. “I’m afraid I don’t have any food for you, little fella,” he told the cat. The cat tilted its head, apparently confused by the interaction. He put it down, smiled, and gave it one final scratch on the head before leaving.

Noelan walked back to the car and slid back into the driver’s seat, his expression neutral as he adjusted the rearview mirror. The failed robber shifted in the backseat; his bound hands denied any comfort.

“So,” he ventured, voice tinged with desperation, “you’re not actually taking me to jail, right? Because then she’s good as gone.”

Noelan chuckled and exchanged a glance with Lawson. “No jail. Not yet.”

Relief softened Bennet’s face. Hands (still bound behind his back) rose hopeful. Pathetic but hopeful. “Then... can I make tracks?”

“Not so fast.” Lawson twisted in his seat to look back at him. “How exactly are you planning on getting that money?”

Noelan didn’t say anything and simply glared at him through the rearview mirror.

“Suppose I could get my piece back?” he muttered after a moment. “I’d still have time to…”

“Wise up,” Lawson said. “You really think we’d let you go rob someone else?”

Noelan scoffed. “Not a chance, jackass.”

“But my fiancée!” Bennet’s voice cracked.

Something cold crossed Noelan’s expression. “Is this fiancée of yours close by? Where’s she staying?”

“Huh?” Bennet asked.

“Where does your broad live?” Noelan said slowly, enunciating every word, like he was talking to a slow child, or a sputtering suspect. “We’ll head there now. Protect your little princess.”

Bennet opened his mouth to spit out some excuse, but the words got somehow stuck when he caught Noelan’s glare. He slumped back in his seat, suddenly cooperative.

“Hang a left up past them elk, over there.” Despite the chill, sweat was beading Bennet’s brow.

Chapter 2

Their car’s engine bellowed at the still homes of Pine Ridge; the houses gazed back, window curtains drawn like confessional screens, guarding sins and secrets.

Lawson leaned toward Noelan, keeping his voice low. “You sure about this? Shouldn’t we get briefed by Chief Travis, first?”

Noelan smiled. “Sometimes, the best way to resolve a mystery is to be quick about it.”

Bennet answered, “There is no mystery. Danger, for sure, but no mystery.”

His words met silence in the front.

Later, as they turned down a side street, Bennet’s nervous voice broke the silence. “Blue house on the left. But I’m tellin’ you straight: these cats don’t fool around. You don’t know what kind of heat you’re stepping into.”

Noelan and Lawson approached the off-blue abode with Bennet shuffling behind. Reaching the front door, Noelan turned to Bennet and outstretched his palm, “Keys?”

Bennet shrugged. “Oh, no one ’round here locks up. Not ’til after nightfall.”

Noelan opened the door. A tidy, modest entryway led to a small living room. A console table and coat rack stood against one wall, pastel floral wallpaper fading slightly at the seams. The space was clean and well-maintained but lacked personal touches or charm.

“Where is she?” Noelan asked firmly.

“I don’t know. She’s usually here,” Bennet replied, then called out, “Honey!?”

Silence answered him. A Norman Rockwell calendar on the wall had circled October dates in a variety of blue, red, and green.

Bennet exhaled. “She’s probably at her friend’s place. Maybe we should come back later...”

Before Bennet could finish, Noelan was already making his way to the kitchen. Lawson frowned disapprovingly.

“Make yourself at home, why don’t you?” Bennet muttered, following with Lawson slowly behind.

Noelan opened the fridge, closed it, then opened it again, expecting better food to materialize somehow. He finally abandoned the fridge and snagged an apple from a fruit bowl.

“Hey, are you seriously going to keep me tied up in my lady’s house?” Bennet asked.

“Is this even her house?” Lawson asked, doubtful.

Noelan unsheathed a throwing knife from under his coat and lifted it into a sunbeam seeping through the kitchen window. He turned the blade slowly, smiling at the gleam.

Noelan’s stare moved, deliberate, like a finger laid on a trigger… and met Bennet’s.

Without a word, still holding that stare, he carved the apple, precise, unhurried.

The shape that emerged was a bunny.

Bennet gulped.

Noelan set the tiny rabbit upright. “So, when’s the wedding?”

“Yeah. Better hope you booked the church basement, mope,” Lawson added.

Bennet, stammering, said, “Well, we’re not officially engaged yet, but… we’ve been together for years and I was gonna propose tomorrow at the festival.”

The sound of a doorknob turning cut him off. They stood in silence. A woman’s laughter drifted in, followed by a masculine chuckle.

“Oh, Jim,” the woman’s voice purred, “you’re such a scoundrel. What if Bennet came by?”

“Why would he? He never comes ‘round in the afternoon, does he?” Jim oozed. “We’ve got time, baby.”

Bennet’s face sank, like a ball and chain tossed overboard into the sea, as his lass got passionately kissed and fondled by his best friend.

Noelan snickered. Lawson smirked.

The woman locked onto Bennet’s bound hands, then flicked to Noelan’s knife. Her expression raced from corybantic confusion to harebrained horror. Finally, she fainted. The THUD that her face made bouncing off the wooden floor shattered the scene’s awkward silence.

Cheating-Culprit-Jim froze like... like something cold... that freezes really fast. Tall, gruff, handsome, and just standing there, mouth agape, eyes darting from his fainted lover, to Bennet bawling, to a knife-wielding stranger, to a bunny apple, back to the dame, and to the knife again, before regaining his composure, bolting out the door, and bolting the door behind him.

Tears of rage and heartbreak slid down Bennet’s face. Lawson, perturbed by the slobbery emotions, almost wanted to pat Bennet’s shoulder, but didn’t.

Meanwhile, Noelan snagged a polka-dotted kitchen towel, wiped the blade down, then deftly slipped it home inside his jacket.

“I can’t believe she would... and with Jim of all people!” Bennet blubbered out between sobs.

Noelan cleared his throat. “Not exactly a surprise, though. You were saying she might get grabbed,” he said with a slight scoff.

“Not like that! Not by HIM!”

A soft groan from the floor drew their attention. The puddle of a woman was coalescing.

“Looks like she’s done with her nap,” Lawson observed.

Noelan offered the plate with the apple bunny on it to Bennet. “Here you go, lad,” Noelan said flatly. “Maybe you two can still work things out.”

Bennet, hands still bound in duct tape, ignored the plate.

“To hell with her,” Bennet spat. “Let her get kidnapped. In fact, let her die!”

The woman, now fully awake, pushed herself up from the floor. As Bennet’s words registered, her face rotted from shame to affront. She knew in her soul that she was the victim here.

Here come the waterworks.

She crossed to Bennet in two strides, wound up, and slapped him hard across his face.

“You monster!” she cried, then stormed towards the front door. Finding it locked, she pirouetted, marched back to Bennet, and roughly searched his pockets. House keys nabbed, she looked at him once more, and seemed, for just a moment, to consider not slapping him again.

SLAP!

STOMP, STOMP, STOMP, STOMP!

SLAM!

and then the clicks of the door locking behind her.

Lawson sighed. “Let’s go, Noelan. This four-flusher already lied about his girlfriend being a fiancée. He’s crookeder than my golf slice.”

Noelan nodded and tossed the plate with the apple bunny on the kitchen counter. He approached the door and tried the knob. It didn’t budge.

Bennet opened his mouth, about to suggest using his key, when he realized his Jezebel had taken it.

Noelan twisted the knob, pulled upward and punched with his open palm like he was performing some kind of voodoo doorknob surgery. Naturally, the door swung open.

“How did you…”

“Trade secret,” Noelan replied curtly, already heading for the car.

While settling into the front seat, Bennet’s panicked voice came from the doorway of the little blue house. “Hey, wait a minute! Cut this duct tape off of me before you go!”

Lawson glanced at Noelan. “He’ll figure it out.”

Noelan started the engine. “Yeah. Let’s roll.”

Chapter 3

Their journey-dirt-stained car barreled down the small town street, leaving behind an untrustable scallywag who just had his own trust broken (presumably) by his not-yet-fiancée and his “best friend” Jim. Bennet might be pondering all that while he chewed off the duct tape binding his hands — or maybe not. Either way, Noelan’s eyes were fixed on the road ahead, while Lawson absently tapped his fingers on the dashboard.

Lawson broke the silence first. “Small town, small problems. Kind of figures.”

Noelan chuckled, “Didn’t figure it’d be nothing but hijinks, though.”

“To the police station then?”

Noelan nodded. “Their Chief, Travis, sent the request for Agency juice. He’d better be able to give us the skinny on any local shakedown rackets, and the tidbits that didn’t make the file about the disappearances.”

Their car pulled up in front of a brick building with an American flag on the pole.

The lot held five vehicles, none of them ambitious. A mud-caked Ford pickup. A Chevy Bel Air with a cracked taillight. The department’s sole patrol car, which looked like it had lost an argument with a mailbox. A station wagon the color of surrender. And a rust-bucket International Harvester that probably hadn’t left that spot since Truman.

Then, parked at the end like it had wandered in from a different galaxy, a spanking-new 1956 Cadillac Eldorado Biarritz. Mandan Red. Gold grille. Gold Sabre wheels. Shark fins that could cut sunshine in half. Mud on the whitewalls, mud splashed up behind the rear fenders, the kind of mud you pick up on mountain roads, or off them.

The metal sign out front swore that this was, indeed, the Pine Ridge Police Station.

As they stepped out of the car, Noelan straightened his tie. “Let’s see how much they have figured out.”

They breathed in one more lovely breath of crisp mountain air.

Fumes of cigarettes and stale coffee, rocket fuel for fish story swappin’, mugged them as they walked in. A junior cop pecked slow, real slow, on the typewriter:

click... click-click... DING!... click........... click.

County maps covered one wall, dotted with colored pins and a legend in the corner: blue marking seasonal fishing violations, green property disputes, yellow mysterious road poop sightings, and white phantom noise complaints.

No pins for missing persons, though.

Over at the front desk, a young officer, nametagged Guss Williams, looked up from his paperwork, his eyebrows trying to crisscross and lips puckering just a bit at the sight of the trouble that just walked in.

Noelan approached. “Good afternoon. Here to speak with Chief Travis.”

The officer snorted. “Is that right, city slicker? Well, I’m here waiting for the Queen. What do you want with the Chief?”

Noelan pulled a folded document from his inner pocket. He unfolded it and held up some official-looking paper bearing the police chief’s signature and seal. “Here on Chief Travis’s request,” Noelan said. “Additional assistance was deemed necessary by the Agency.”

Officer Guss stood up like his chair had zapped his rear-end and looked from the paper to Noelan. “Well, uh, why didn’t you say so? I think the Chief’s in his office. I’ll let him know,” he said, then scurried off.

Noelan started, a little under his breath: “You think maybe…”

But was cut off by Lawson, “Not here. Not now.”

Instead, they stood there, clocking that map and enduring the occasional typewriter pecking.

Eventually, Guss came back. “Actually, the Chief ain’t here right now, but Captain Gill said to show you in. Please, follow me.” Then muttered as they started, “sorry about the rough greeting back there.”

They were led down a short hallway to Captain Gill’s office. Guss rapped twice on the door before opening it.

What a room. The desk, if one was somewhere under that mini-mountain range of files, took up center stage. The brown outline of a flask bottom stained the top of one of the folders. An overflowing glass ashtray hid behind two cups of mostly chewed-up pencils and pens. Crumbs that had escaped the wax paper from a half-eaten sandwich rallied around a cream ceramic coffee cup with the word “Vote” on it.

The framed picture on the wall was a younger Gill, in an army uniform, bronze caduceus pin on the collar, standing alongside other soldiers stationed somewhere in the Pacific, perhaps Guam.

He sat behind his desk, jacket draped over his chair, sleeves rolled up to his elbows.

There were two chairs set in front of Gill’s desk. Noelan took a seat. Lawson didn’t.

“Good afternoon,” Gill began. He pushed aside a stack of files to clear space on his desk. “I apologize for Chief Travis’s absence. He’s been called away on urgent business. But I understand you’re here about our situation.”

Noelan nodded and placed his card on the desk.

Gill picked up the card and looked at Noelan. “Noelan Elyrite? Heard you died in a blaze of glory back in that Eastern Empire incident. Newspapers said you single-handedly dismantled some corrupt monarchy there?”

“They exaggerate when they’re not just making stuff up.” Noelan half-smiled as he glanced at Lawson. “The Agency isn’t always as dramatic as the papers make us out to be.”

Lawson tilted his head back. “Some of us prefer a more measured approach.”

“Well,” Gill said, “I’m glad the Agency sent you. We could use your expertise. I assume you’ve been briefed on the disappearances?”

In the corner, a dead moth dangled from a cobweb.

“Only the basics.” Noelan began unwrapping a plum candy. “Would appreciate your insights.”

The Captain cleared his throat. “Over the past two months, we’ve had seven disappearances. All connected, we believe, to Irving Falkner.”

Gill let the name hang for a moment before continuing. “Head of the Trash collector’s union. He runs the city’s waste management. But we suspect the man deals with Pine Ridge garbage in more ways than one.”

“Got a photo of Falker?”

“Uhh… didn’t think we needed it. Everyone knows Falkner. But, umm, we do, of course, have files on the missing persons.”

Gill picked up a stack of photos on his desk and placed each down as he spoke. “We’ve noticed a pattern in the disappearances: Every victim seems like the type who would either owe money or pose a threat to his operations.”

Noelan popped the candy in his mouth. “Interesting…”

Gill pulled open the desk drawer and retrieved a manila folder. “Now, this may just be a dumb cop-hunch, but my gut tells me that mayor Coolidge is wrapped up in all this, somehow.”

Lawson furrowed his brows. “How so?”

Gill opened the folder. “Falkner gets regular grants from the mayor’s office. In return, he’s quite generous with campaign contributions.”

Noelan shifted in his seat to get a better view of the documents, the leather squeaking beneath him. “But no bodies?”

“That’s just it,” Gill muttered as he tugged at fist-full of hair on the top of his own head. “People vanish, but we never find remains. No evidence of murder, just... gone.”

“Like they’ve been cleaned up?” Noelan added. “Professional-like?”

“You bet,” said the captain with a smile.

“Plausible theory, but blind theories won’t cut it in court,” Lawson said.

A loud silence hung in the room.

Gill coughed slightly. “Anyway, we need someone who can...” he paused, looked thoughtfully to the side, then said, “operate with more flexibility than we can. Which is why we requested your help.”

Noelan sat back, folded his hands behind his head, and stared off at the ceiling. “If the crime lord were to... accidentally die tomorrow, would you…”

“No.” Lawson and Gill interrupted in unison. Lawson shot Noelan a warning glance. Captain Gill looked genuinely shocked at the suggestion, though maybe he shouldn’t have.

Noelan smiled and held up his hands. “I was kidding. But seriously… if we get evidence of the crime lord’s illegal activities, would you be able to deal with him?”

“That’s all we’re asking for,” Gill exhaled, releasing the tension in his shoulders.

Noelan pushed himself up from his chair. He stepped toward the door, but at the hall threshold, he turned back, “There were no pins on the map out front for the missing people. Why’s that?”

Gill turned his palms up and offered with a smile, “If we knew where to put the pins, they wouldn’t be missing, would they?”

Walking down the corridor, Noelan nearly stepped on a paperclip twisted into a star glinting on the floor. When they stepped out of the police station, Noelan glanced at Lawson. “Gill seems competent.”

“A little too polished if you ask me,” Lawson added. “I wonder what he’s not telling us.”

Chapter 4

Outside the police station, Pine Ridge Lake stretched out beyond the town. Though the sun had already dipped behind the mountains on the far shore, across the lake, reflections of gold, pink, and purple mirrored the clouds in the sky.

Noelan slid into the driver’s seat and said, “Let’s grab some grub before we turn in.”

“There’s gotta be a place nearby...” Lawson said while checking the map.

Driving through narrow streets, they happened upon a bar tucked away in some side street. The cheerful notes of an acoustic guitar and a lone singer’s voice drifted out whenever the door swung open, along with bursts of conversation and laughter.

“Well, this looks promising,” Noelan said as they parked.

Inside the joint, pine tables and pine floors, pine stools and a pine bar. The footrest, at least, was made of worn brass.

The smell of fried food and beer mingled with the sounds of jovial conversations as the guitarist in the corner sang his way through a ballad, his fingers picking out melodies on steel strings.

Noelan and Lawson found a table near the back wall. Noelan positioned himself to keep the entrance in view. A waitress approached.

“Good evening. What can I get you?” she asked with a pencil poised over her notepad.

“I’ll have the shepherd’s pie and…” Noelan glanced at Lawson.

“Just water.”

“Two waters,” Noelan adjusted smoothly.

The waitress arched an eyebrow, her pencil hesitating over the notepad before she nodded, “You got it,” then left.

“The crime lord’s operation seems sophisticated,” Noelan said, keeping his voice low. “And that business with Bennet this morning... something doesn’t add up.”

Lawson leaned back. “I wouldn’t stake that lowlife’s yarns against a wooden nickel. Sure, Falkner sounds suspicious; but the mayor’s involvement... what, is every numbskull from top to bottom crooked in Pine Ridge? We can’t go off half-cocked on just small-town gossip and dime-store campaign donations.”

“Always by the book,” Noelan muttered, but nodded. “We’ll dig deeper tomorrow.”

Their waters arrived, along with the plate of food Noelan had ordered. As he took a bite, Lawson tilted his head. “That tune... doesn’t it sound familiar?”

Noelan turned toward the music. The guitarist in the corner wasn’t just some local talent filling space. No. Calloused fingers owned that stage. His fingers flew, flew, flew! across the strings. Come to think of it, there was a certain haunting familiarity to the tune that tugged at memories long stored away.

Then it clicked. Noelan’s face lit up like a match struck in some forgotten mineshaft, followed by his first genuine smile in what felt like weeks. “That’s Eugene!” he said. His voice brimmed with fond nostalgia.

“Eugene Goodhart?” Lawson asked. “What in tarnation is he doing in Pine Ridge?”

With Eugene’s guitar pick picking up the pace, the song built to its crescendo. As the final note faded, the crowd erupted in enthusiastic applause. Eugene stood and offered a bow that somehow managed to be both cockamamie and charming. There he basked in the glow of the crowd until he noticed Noelan. Then his jaw dropped in dramatic surprise.

“Ladies and gentlemen, you’ve been wonderful, but I’m afraid I’ve just spotted an old friend who owes me several drinks and at least one good explanation!” Eugene announced to scattered laughter. He carefully set his guitar in its case and made his way through the crowd, accepting compliments with humble poise. He reached Noelan with a broad grin and delivered a hearty clap on his shoulder.

“Noelan Elyrite,” Eugene said, dropping into a chair. “Still solving everything with elaborate schemes?”

“I have no idea what you’re talking about,” Noelan said, not holding back the smile.

“Would someone please bring this man a whiskey! And a beer for me. I’ve earned it after that set.”

The waitress (Kate, according to her name tag) appeared with remarkable timing. As she set down their drinks, Eugene turned back to Noelan. “Man, seeing you here reminds me of old times. Remember that summer when you decided to be a detective?”

“We agreed never to mention that.”

The waitress let out an “Ooooh! Sounds like this’ll be juicy!”

“Oh lord,” Eugene took a gulp from his beer, grinning. “Summer of ’43. Noelan here decides he’s going to be a detective.”

“I was conducting an investigation,” Noelan corrected primly.

“Into missing pies. From window sills.”

“There was a pattern.”

“He maps out every missing pie in three counties,” Eugene continued. “Makes this crazy wall of string and newspaper clippings…”

“It was a legitimate crime board.”

“For pies, Noelan. For pies.”

Kate started laughing as she glanced down at the shepherd’s pie she had just delivered. Noelan sighed into his whiskey.

“So, this genius,” Eugene pointed at his friend, “decides the culprit is hitting Mrs. Tolkien’s prize-winning apple pie next. Sets up a whole stakeout. Convinces me to hide in the bushes with him at midnight…”

Lawson pinched the bridge of his nose. “Your methodology hasn’t changed much, has it?”

“The evidence was solid.”

“And sure enough,” Eugene was full-on grinning now, “someone big can be seen moving in the dark. Like Flash Gordon, Noelan jumps out, waving his dad’s old deputy badge frantically above his head…”

“Which I had requisitioned,” Noelan muttered.

“Stolen.”

“Temporarily requisitioned without authorization.”

“And finds himself face to face with Matilda.”

“Matilda?” Kate asked.

“Old and gray. Wrinkled. Huge ears.” Eugene paused for effect, before bursting out with, “And a Trunk!”

“What?!? To put the pies in or something?” asked the confused waitress.

“Carson Brothers Circus’ most talented elephant,” Noelan said with surprising dignity. “She had developed a fondness for baked goods during her evening constitutionals.”

“The elephant had escaped?” Kate gasped.

“Oh no,” Eugene exclaimed, wiping away tears of laughter. “They were letting her exercise at night to avoid the heat. Her trainer was right there, about to die laughing at this skinny kid trying to arrest his elephant for pie theft.”

“I was going to issue a citation,” Noelan corrected.

“Of course you were,” scoffed Lawson.

“To an elephant,” added Eugene.

“She was a repeat offender.”

“The best part,” Eugene could barely get the words out, “was Noelan trying to figure out how to get the damn handcuffs on! While Matilda just kept on...” He dissolved into laughter.

“She was eating the evidence,” Noelan admitted, finally cracking a smile. “Rather messily. Had to throw that shirt away.”

“That was his favorite shirt! The trainer felt so bad,” Eugene continued, “that he taught us how to sneak into the circus for free. Which is how we ended up…”

“No,” Noelan cut him off. “That’s a story we’re never telling.”

“Aw come on,” Kate pleaded. “You can’t stop there!”

“Let’s just say,” Eugene stage-whispered, “that Noelan’s talent for disguise started early. And he made a very convincing…”

Noelan cleared his throat loudly. “I still have that badge, Gene.”

“You mean you still have evidence of theft of police property?” Eugene asked, donning a shocked expression. Then, wagging his finger, he continued, “My, my, what would your superiors say?”

“Probably the same thing they’ve been saying for years,” Lawson said with a sigh.

“I’m sure they’d want answers to the same question I’ve been pondering for all these years,” Noelan replied in earnest. “How exactly did an elephant eat six pies without leaving a single crumb on the plates?”

“So, you think someone else was in on the elephant pie eating conspiracy?” Eugene asked.

“Yes! A pie-eating-elephant conspiracy. Isn’t it obvious?” Noelan said, triumphantly raising his fist.

A call from another table pulled Kate away, still giggling. She could have sworn she saw Noelan’s shoulders shaking with suppressed laughter as she left.

“That was perfect,” Eugene said, then cracked up again. “Except for the apple-pie all over Mr. Future Agent’s favorite shirt. You’ve always been...”

“Determined? Methodical? A credit to law enforcement?” Noelan suggested.

“A complete pain in the ass who couldn’t leave a mystery alone,” Eugene corrected cheerfully.

“Some things never change,” Lawson added with familiar resignation.

“Speaking of mysteries, what brings you to Pine Ridge?” Noelan asked, leaning in. “Last I heard, you were playing concert halls in Europe.”

Eugene’s smile faded slightly. “Ah, well. My sister. She’s gotten herself into trouble. Again.”

“Gambling?”

“Always those damn ponies,” Eugene sighed in frustration. “This time it’s different, though. Either she owes money to the local crime lord, or she’s paying him protection money to keep other creditors away.” He shrugged but couldn’t quite hide his worry. “Either way, I’ve gotta find her and help her out of whatever trouble she’s in.”

Noelan nodded solemnly as he reached into his coat pocket. Eugene smiled again when he saw Noelan unwrapping a plum candy. “Still have a sweet tooth, huh?”

“He’s been eating too many of those,” Lawson observed dryly.

“So, what do you know about the crime lord?” Noelan asked, popping the candy in his mouth.

Eugene leaned back in his chair. “Not much about the man himself, but his goons are hard to miss.”

“What do they look like?”

“Doesn’t matter what they look like.” Eugene waved dismissively. “It’s about presence. You know ’em the moment they walk in.” His voice took on a suspenseful tone. “Conversations die mid-sentence, the room goes quiet, folks act like they’ve got the heebie-jeebies...”

A reckless grin spread across his face. “Though next time I see ‘em, I’ll show them who’s boss and teach ’em another lesson.”

Noelan laughed slightly. “Eugene, what do you mean by ‘another’ lesson?”

The door burst open with a heavy creak. The three of them turned towards the entrance.

“Come on. It can’t be,” Lawson breathed.

Well, it was.

In walked four men whose knuckles were headlines for violence. The bar’s friendly chatter choked and died. Screeching scrapes of chairs replaced the rowdy laughter as patrons maneuvered to make themselves smaller.

Noelan twitched toward his holster, but Lawson’s steel-cold glare froze him mid-reach.

“Speak of the devils,” Eugene whispered as he dove under the table.

Just as some customers were making tracks for the door, one of the knuckle-sandwich men shouted, “Make sure you settle up before you scurry off!” His voice carried the kind of authority that suggested breaking a leg wasn’t just a stage metaphor.

Soon, only a handful of patrons remained. The gangsters ordered whiskey and began to talk among themselves.

“That wise guy with the guitar’s got some nerve,” the bird’s-nest-bearded goon said. “Making us look like chumps in front of everybody...”

When the bartender returned with their drinks, the leader leaned in. “Say, you seen a mug around here, brown hair, plays guitar, thinks he’s real clever?”

The bartender nodded towards Noelan’s table. Noelan silently shook his head.

“...Can’t say that I have,” the bartender replied after a careful pause.

“Next time I see that punk, I’m gonna…”

A soft humming suddenly came from under the table.

“Shut him up,” Lawson whispered urgently.

Noelan knocked over his glass. The crash was followed by a loud cough, a soft kick under the table, and Eugene’s muffled grunt.

The gangsters whirled around. “What was that?”

“Clumsy of me,” Noelan said drunkenly. “Must’ve had a few too many.”

“Pull yourself together, man,” Lawson improvised.

The men stared for a moment longer before turning back. “This job’s making me jumpy,” the Bird’s-Nest-Beard muttered. “Starting to hear that damn tune everywhere.”

As they settled back into their drinks, their conversation shifted. “Most of you boys got tomorrow off for the festival, right?” the leader asked.

“Not me,” said an animated wiry man. “Got assigned to Mr. Falkner’s little gathering.”

The others turned to him. “Rough luck,” Mr. Bird’s-Nest said, but the wiry man’s frown curved into a smug smile.

“Oh yeah, real rough,” he drawled. “Crystal glasses, proper silverware, beautiful dames,” he added with a wink, “booty for the bold. Even heard the lady mayor’s gonna be there.”

“Listen to this hotshot,” the quiet one snorted. “Getting too big for his britches ’cause he gets to carry trays for the swells.”

Under the table, Noelan could hear a muffled laugh.

“Document everything,” Lawson murmured. “This could be important.”

Noelan nodded slightly.

“Ain’t like that,” the wiry Booty Hunter said. “But tomorrow, while you boys are out there licking cotton candy, I’ll be sampling the finest champagne in Pine Ridge and eating the lady mayor’s famous pie.”

The four laughed.

“Keep bragging,” Mr. Bird’s-Nest warned, “and you’ll be eating through a straw, proper silverware or not.”

The conversation drifted until the bartender approached. “Last call,” the bartender announced. “Closing early tonight on account of the festival tomorrow.”

The men grumbled but finished their drinks. They tossed some change on the bar and started out the door, but the leader waited there at the bar. “If you see that guitar player,” he said quietly, “tell him Mr. Falkner’s getting tired of his little song and dance. It’s for his own good.”

When he left, Noelan leaned down. “Coast is clear.”

Eugene crawled out from under the table, his clothes soaked with spilled beer. Without missing a beat (Eugene was a musician, after all) he launched into his victory song:

The tough guys are starting to sway,
Their fierce scowls melting away,
They can huff and puff,
Act ever so tough,
But I’m still singing my songs today!

His impromptu performance was met with laughter from Noelan and Lawson, and even a chuckle from the bartender who approached their table shaking his head.

“You’ve got spirit, son,” the bartender said. “Just hope it don’t get you killed.”

“Life’s too short not to sing,” Eugene replied with a wink. “Singing’s what I was born to do, and one of the many things I love about life”

Noelan settled up and left a generous tip. “For the disruption,” he explained.

“And the discretion,” Lawson added.

The bartender pocketed the money with a nod. “Come back anytime, gentlemen. Though maybe when your other friends ain’t around, eh?”

Outside, the cool night air was a refreshing change from the stuffy bar. Eugene turned to Noelan. “I’m crashing at my sister’s place, hoping she’ll just happen to show up. You staying at the Pine Ridge Inn?

“That’s right,” Noelan nodded.

“Good choice,” Eugene grinned. “Food’s great, and the view from the rooftop at sunset...” He kissed his fingers like a chef. “Perfect spot to practice guitar for a small, intimate audience.”

“How do you know that?” Lawson chuckled.

Noelan also laughed.

“Well, I should get going. Stay out of trouble!” He walked backwards a few steps, still grinning. “Or at least invite me along if you don’t!”

Noelan watched as Eugene disappeared into the darkness, raggedy-old guitar case swinging jauntily.

Back in the car, Noelan reached for the ignition, but Lawson cleared his throat. “Aren’t you forgetting something?”

Noelan nodded and pulled a small notebook from his coat pocket. The scratch of his pencil filled the quiet car as he scribbled down the night's loose ends.

His handwriting grew messier as his mind wandered to possibilities. “The party at the crime lord’s mansion...” Noelan mused. “It’s an opportunity we can’t ignore.”

“Oh, here we go…” Lawson sighed from the passenger seat.

Noelan unwrapped a plum candy and popped it into his mouth. The sweetness made him smile.

“We could infiltrate as servers,” Noelan suggested. “See if we can find out how deeply the mayor’s involved.”

“We can’t go in all loosey-goosey,” Lawson laughed. “The evidence wouldn’t be admissible without a warrant.”

“…Yeah, you’re right.” Noelan said. But he didn’t laugh.

The drive back to the inn was short. The streets were dead. By the time Noelan pulled into the inn’s parking spot, the candy’s sweetness had faded. It left behind a subtle but bitter aftertaste.

The porch lights of Pine Ridge Inn cast a warm glow into the autumn night. As Noelan climbed the wooden steps, a fluffy white and gray cat weaved between his feet. It nearly tripped him.

“Careful there.” Noelan scooped up the little cat. It settled contentedly in his arms, purring as he scratched behind its ears.

Inside, the inn was cozy and quiet except for the crackling of the hearth fire. The empty tables dreamed of tomorrow’s conversations. The chairs were tucked in for the night.

The Innkeeper stood near a window, tending to a small tree. The snip of his scissors was precise and methodical as he removed spent blooms.

“Evening,” he greeted warmly, but did not look up from his work. “How are you finding Pine Ridge so far?”

“Why’s he cutting the flowers?” Lawson asked quietly.

Noelan softly shushed Lawson, before replying, “The town is gorgeous; you’re lucky to live here.”

“Sure. It has its advantages over big city hustle and bustle, but there are always tradeoffs.”

“If you don’t mind me asking,” Noelan said, still petting the cat in his arms, “what are you doing with the plant?”

“Ah, this?” The Innkeeper held up his scissors. “It’s called deadheading. Essential for the overall health of the plant. By removing these withered blooms, the bonsai can focus its energy on other growth, producing more leaves, strengthening its root system.” He set another dried flower aside. “Nature knows what it’s doing, but sometimes it needs a helping hand.”

“Fascinating,” Noelan said, unenthralled.

“Are you gonna join the fun tomorrow night?” the Innkeeper asked. He reached for his watering can then added, “Small town festivals give you city folk a wholesome way to see what life’s all about.”

Lawson nodded approvingly. “Oh, maybe that would be…”

Noelan scoffed and chuckled slightly, “Yeah, no. Catch you later, guy.”

“Of course, of course. Rest well.” The Innkeeper returned to his gardening.

Noelan set the cat down. It wound around his ankles once before padding away. As Noelan climbed the stairs, the peaceful sounds of the inn followed him up: the gentle snip of scissors, the tick of a clock, the soft creak of new-ish floorboards. Even Lawson seemed content to let the quiet of the evening settle around them.

Tomorrow morning, everything would begin in earnest. But right then, in that moment, the world felt properly ordered and safe.

Chapter 5

A sharp knock jolted Noelan from sleep. He reached for the gun under his pillow before his eyes were fully open.

“At this hour?” Noelan muttered. The pale dawn light snuck through the curtains onto the wood floor.

“Could just be housekeeping,” Lawson remarked.

“Could also be the Satin Scorpion,” Noelan whispered. “Or maybe The Porcelain Viper finally tracked me down.”

Lawson stood near the window. “How many vengeful criminals do you know who knock?”

“The polite ones do.” Noelan moved toward the door, keeping to the wall. “Remember that Ivory Butcher in Vienna? And The Porcelain Viper always did have peculiar manners for a hitman.”

“Please don’t shoot any maids this time,” Lawson exhorted.

The knock came again, rapid now, almost frantic. Noelan pressed himself against the wall beside the door, gun raised. “Who is it?”

“Ro? It’s Eugene.” The voice was slightly muffled, but unmistakable. “Listen, I found something you’re gonna want to see.”

Noelan lowered his revolver and opened the door. Eugene stood there, breathing quickly as if he’d been running, clothes rumpled and pants dirt-streaked. Leaves clung to his hair.

“You look like you’ve been playing leapfrog in a cemetery… and lost,” Noelan remarked.

“Yeah. How’d you manage to lose at leapfrog, Gene?” added Lawson.

“Hardy har har. So early this morning I…”

Noelan glanced down at his undershirt and trousers and cut Eugene off, “Give me a minute.”

Once Noelan was properly dressed, they headed up the corridor and started down the stairs.

“So, there I was this morning, couldn’t sleep, thought I’d take a walk.” Eugene began, his voice low. “Met this charming dog, a golden retriever, absolute poetry on paws. We’re playing fetch, having a grand time, he’s barking, I’m barking, I’m throwin’, he’s fetchin’... all as it should be. Then I wind up and show him what a real stick toss looks like, you know, throw ‘em for a loop. Then, this little trickster one-ups me. Instead of bringing back the stick, he shows up with a damn corndog!”

“A corndog?” Noelan repeated flatly.

“I’m thinking, ‘Well, that’s awful nice of you, good pup, sharing your breakfast,’ but I wasn’t feeling particularly hungry. Anyway, he starts pulling at my sleeve, like he wants to show me something.”

They reached the bottom of the stairs, entering the main sitting area. The Innkeeper sat in his usual spot, newspaper raised like a shield against the morning sunrise. A coffee cup steamed beside him.

“So, I followed him and found a body!” Eugene said, then immediately clamped his mouth shut when he noticed the Innkeeper.

“A body of water,” Noelan said quickly. “A pond. For fishing.”

“Yes! Yes, exactly,” Eugene nodded vigorously. “Quite the fishing spot. Crystal clear water, really... dead quiet there.”

“Smooth. Real smooth,” Lawson muttered.

The Innkeeper lowered his paper slightly. “Good morning.”

“Morning,” Noelan replied, relieved.

“Thanks for telling me the room number,” Eugene said quickly. “Psst, we should scram before I spill any tea on this fine inn’s floor.”

Eugene mouthed ‘sorry’ as they made their way to the front door. Noelan glanced at the porch railing as they stepped outside. The little gray and white cat wasn’t in its usual spot. Somehow, its absence made the morning feel less peaceful.

A golden retriever waited at the bottom of the steps, tail wagging when it recognized Eugene.

“There’s my good boy!” Eugene called out as he descended to scratch the dog’s ears. Despite its dirty coat, the retriever carried itself with a certain dignity... well, for a dog.

“That’s some pooch. Real class act,” Lawson noted.

“Yeah,” Noelan agreed. “Now, where exactly is it?”

“It’s not far,” Eugene replied. “This way. Follow me,” he said as he jogged off like a kid to a playground.

The morning sun dappled through autumn leaves still clinging to branches, while their shoes crunched and crackled the fallen leaves. A Steller’s Jay witnessed it all, nodding along and providing commentary the only way she knew how, with a “shaak, shaak, shaak!”

“Lovely morning for a jog, really,” Eugene remarked.

“…or to find a corpse, I suppose,” offered Lawson.

The dog stopped suddenly; tail dropped. It looked back at Eugene, then forward again, whining softly.

“It’s OK, boy. It’s OK.” Eugene knelt beside the dog, then looked up at Noelan. “We’re close. He started acting funny around here last time.”

Noelan looked in the direction the dog had been looking. “This way? How far, Gene?”

“I don’t know. It’s…” The dog whined again. Eugene picked up the retriever’s front paws and said, “Don’t worry boy! I’ve got you. Know what? I think I’ll call you Gogh Getter. Because you went and got…”

“Hold on,” Noelan interrupted.

Up ahead, above leaves and pine needles, mounds of dirt and clumps of earth scattered the way only an animal scatters it. He’d get there. But something flapped against a chain-link fence to his left, a tiny motion in his periphery, and his feet were already carrying him there.

A scrap of olive green cloth, maybe two inches square, snagged on a twist of wire near the top of the fence. The chain link wasn’t that old, but a smidgen of rust bled orange where the wire bent. Noelan checked the ground around the fence. No prints, no scuff marks, just leaves lying the way leaves lie when nobody’s stepped on them.

He worked the cloth free, careful not to tear it further. Noelan examined it. Tight weave. Heavier than it looked. And, right there, along one of the edges, ran two silver threads.

He headed for the dirt mounds.

The Steller’s Jay from earlier, or maybe a different one, sang from somewhere in the trees. The trees didn’t care. The sun kept shining. Everything in these woods was having a wonderfully pleasant morning except for whatever was under that dirt.

The dog had done real work here. Out from the soil, pale against dark soil, an arm jutted out.

Exposed from the elbow, the hand was open, reaching like it had one last thing to say. Noelan stood there for a moment watching a carpenter ant crawl down the arm, farther and farther, trying to calculate if it would march over the space where a finger should have been.

“Let’s get a kit from the station,” Lawson said. “Brushes, at least.”

But Noelan was already squatting, already swatting dirt off the corpse’s shoulder. “If this town has a forensics kit, I’ll eat my hat.”

“You’re not wearing a hat,” Eugene said.

Noelan stopped mid-brush. “That’s because I ate it last mission.”

He brushed his palms together, casual, quick, ineffective. Once you get your hands dirty, they’re not getting clean.

He whipped out a notepad and started sketching the subject’s features.

“Male, probably mid-thirties,” Lawson said. “Clearly not an animal attack. One suspect cleared,” he said, eyeing the dog suspiciously.

“These cuts...” Noelan pointed with his pencil. “Meant for pain? And not a quick death? Medieval inquisitors used to cut like that to tug on the entrails to extract confessions.”

“Was this to get information?” Lawson asked quietly. “Or for the love of the torture?”

“Maybe it was something else,” Noelan said. He pulled back the dirty, button-up shirt. “The chest cavity... see these incisions? Dollars to donuts he’s missing organs.”

“Like that Organ Reaper, back in Chicago,” Lawson murmured. “Pretty sure that case went cold, too.”

Eugene had been quiet. “My sister... she’s been missing for a week now. She said she’d meet me for dinner a few days ago but never showed up. You don’t think...” He gestured at the body.

“Still more likely she just met up with some Casanova and ran off to the beach in Mexico for a couple of weeks, no?” Lawson asked. “Wouldn’t be the first time.”

Eugene just stared at the body, never considering that hopeful possibility.

“We’ll figure out who done it.” Noelan closed his notebook. “This stiff looks like one of the missing persons from Captain Gill’s photos.”

“Time to fill out an STQ report?” Lawson said. “It sure looks connected to the other disappearances.”

“There a phone nearby?” Noelan asked.

Eugene nodded. “There’s a booth just past Kurt Street. So, can we get out of these creepy woods yet?”

“Yeah, the taste is getting to me.”

* * *

The phone booth’s glass fogged with Noelan’s breath. He kept his voice professional, detailing the location and condition of the body. Eugene waited outside (obviously) scratching Gogh Getter behind its gold, fluffy ears. (Can you imagine if he was in the phone booth with the dog and Noelan… and Lawson? That’s just silly.)

The phone booth door squeaked and Noelan stepped out. “They’re sending someone.”

A cold breeze rattled dried leaves across the sidewalk. Gogh Getter trotted alongside Eugene as they walked toward the inn, occasionally sniffing at interesting spots. They passed a yard where a group of kids were playing marbles. The retriever’s ears perked up at their laughter, and without warning, he bounded over at them with an enthusiastic assault… of tail wags and slobbery face licks.

“Hey, wait!” Eugene called after him, but Gogh Getter, already accepting pats from happy children, didn’t even look back.

With a wistful smile, Eugene shrugged. “Must be quite the adventurer in his life.”

A campaign poster fluttered on a shop window: “RE-ELECT COOLIDGE FOR PINE RIDGE.” They rounded the corner to find another poster plastered on a telephone pole: “GILL FOR MAYOR.”

“Interesting,” Lawson mused. “Captain Gill’s suspicions about Coolidge’s dirt just so happens to coincide with his mayoral race against her. Well don’t that beat all?”

Noelan frowned, thinking instead of the current mayor’s meetings with the crime lord, the campaign contributions, and those waste management contracts. Dirty business.

They reached the inn and Noelan noticed that the porch railing still stood empty.

Eugene was already climbing up the steps to the inn, but Noelan hesitated when he saw Lawson stop at the bottom. Halfway up, Eugene paused, glanced at Noelan, then looked away, still waiting there. The air breezed through his hair when he tilted his face toward the sun.

Noelan followed his line of sight and found a lake.

Pine Ridge Lake: simple, bright, pretty. It was dazzling, and looked calm at first glance. But once he looked at it again, more carefully, he realized the waves were sharp, and that the lake wasn’t so placid. But the waves gave it character, and he liked that.

Lawson spoke up. “We should head to the police station.” He gave Noelan a meaningful look. “Someone needs to make sure these small-town boys don’t botch the evidence.”

He leaned closer to Noelan, voice dropping. “Don’t go running off with Eugene chasing ghosts. You saw that body; you know what’s happening to the missing ones.”

“Oh! That’s an idea.” Noelan turned to Eugene. “I’ll go with you, Gene, to look for your sister.”

Eugene’s chin crumpled a bit. Then he said, “Thanks, Ro.”

Lawson quietly sighed.

Noelan watched as Lawson headed toward the station. He waited for a moment more before looking ahead and following Eugene inside.

The inn’s main room was quiet; only a couple of early risers occupied the tables. The scent of butter melting on pancakes and coffee filled the warm air. The Innkeeper looked up from his work behind the counter and offered them a welcoming smile.

“Good morning again, gentlemen. Coffee?”

They settled at a corner table near the window and ordered breakfast. Noelan stirred sugar into his coffee.

“So, I was thinking,” Eugene began, “we should start with Charlotte’s usual spots. The diner where she worked, maybe talk to her friends.” He huddled over his steamy coffee mug. “She does this sometimes, you know? Takes off when the ponies aren’t running her way.”

“But this time it feels different?”

“Yeah,” Eugene said, then thought for a moment. “Well, maybe I’m worrying too much. She knows how to handle herself.” He shrugged slightly. “Last time she disappeared, she turned up in Atlantic City a week later, up three hundred. Still, I thought checking around couldn’t hurt.”

They discussed possible places to look: the horse track, that bar with nickel slots, and a few other dodgy dives she frequented. The Innkeeper remained behind the counter with his newspaper.

As their coffee cups drained away, Eugene’s expression brightened. “Oh! Speaking of places to check: I’m playing in the town square tonight.” He spoke with familiar enthusiasm. “Figured we could meet there around eight. I’ll be done with my set by then, and we can enjoy the festival together.”

Noelan considered, then agreed. “Sounds fun.”

“Right?” Eugene grinned. “Then I’ll see you at the town square this evening?”

Noelan nodded with a genuine smile. Festivals weren’t usually his scene, especially during investigations, but one night couldn’t hurt.

A soft meow made Noelan look around. When he glanced down, he found the little gray and white cat sitting on the floor beside him. Noelan reached down to pet it, and the cat purred and nuzzled right back.

“What’re you up to?” Eugene asked with a mouthful of breakfast.

Noelan withdrew his hand quickly. “Figuring out how we’re going to find your sister.”

Eugene leaned over to peek under the table. “Hmmm,” he said, straightening up and returning to his food.

Noelan glanced down once more at the cat, now curled up contentedly beside his chair, before going back to his own breakfast.

Refilling their cups, the Innkeeper paused and looked at Eugene, “I didn’t get a chance to ask this earlier, but what happened?” He gestured to Eugene’s dirt-streaked clothes. “You look like you’ve been rolling around on the ground.”

Eugene brushed the dirt off his sleeve and rolled it up. There, spread across his forearm, lay a deep purple bruise, yellowing at the edges.

Chapter 6

“You’ve got quite a mark there,” The Innkeeper noticed. He analyzed the bruise. “Ice would help bring down the swelling.”

“Wow, didn’t even notice.” Eugene tugged his sleeve down with an easy smile. “Thanks… uhhh... guess I’ll take a bag of ice if you got one.”

The Innkeeper left, then came back with a bag of ice. Eugene accepted the bag with a quick thanks and pressed it against his wrist.

Breakfast resumed, quiet and comfortable. While Eugene stretched out in his chair with a yawn, Noelan tossed a scrap of bacon to the cat, who ignored it, not being up to standards.

Once they finished eating, Noelan headed to the counter to pay, bag of ice in hand. He placed it on the counter and noticed that jar of plum candies again. The Innkeeper chuckled. “Oh, there’s no limit. It’s good to see folks enjoying these.”

Noelan smiled and pocketed them like some greedy trick or treater. As they headed for the door, Eugene called back, “Great breakfast, by the way!”

Behind them, the Innkeeper hummed softly, straightened the candy jar that didn’t need straightening, then slid back behind his newspaper, headlines heralding the Pine Ridge festival.

* * *

The air was cool. The sun was warm. The lake mirrored the trees. Lovely day for a walk, too, to enjoy the full splendor of Colorado autumn.

And so, they natured their way down the path that wound along the shore of Pine Ridge Lake. Mountain. Lake. Town. Autumn. Perfect. Perfect except for Eugene’s missing sister and that tortured corpse that hung on their minds.

“Alright, time for some investigating,” Noelan said. “Where should we start?”

“The tracks,” Eugene suggested. “She always said that’s where the easy money was.”

“They got the ponies here, too? Remember that time old Daryl Hinckley kicked…”

They spent the morning both reminiscing and asking about Charlotte around town. Most folks just shrugged or shook their heads, but some had more to say.

At the track, a man in a pinstripe suit leaned against the betting window. “Charlotte, eh? Yeah, I know the dame. Smart cookie, but her luck? Hot and cold, if ya catch my drift. Though she ain’t stupid; she always kept square if the vig had muscle. Never missed an envelope, no sir. But other marks?” He straightened his tie with a knowing smirk. “Maybe not as much.”

You meet all kinds when canvassing a small town.

On a street closed off for the upcoming festival, an old lady had Noelan cornered near a sawhorse barricade. She had opinions about pancakes, this woman. Strong opinions. Long opinions. She held Noelan and evangelized about buttermilk and bubbles in the batter. She didn’t blink. Her attention never left his face. Her hands never left his forearm.

Meanwhile, Noelan was acting like if he nodded fast enough she would let him go and leave him be, when the shouting started:

“Twenty bucks! You hear me? Twenty bucks, and she swore it was a sure thing!”

Across the street, a man had Eugene. Not by the arm. By the front of his shirt, bunched in one fist, the other cocked back, and those fists were the size of a baby boulder. The man was tall and wide and angry, and Eugene looked like a coat on a hook.

Noelan removed the woman’s hand from his forearm and walked straight toward the yelling.

“Come by my place tomorrow morning. I’ll make you a whole stack. You look like you could gobble a whole stack,” said the old woman from behind him.

He didn’t look back. The yelling in front of him continued.

“And I ain’t seen a dime of it back. Then I got wind she blew it all on Honey Gallop at forty to one?!? FORTY TO ONE! Sure thing my eye! YOU TELL HER…” then he broke off the moment Noelan arrived.

The thick man let go of Eugene, fixed his collar, and added, voice much softer, “Tell her no IOU this time...” Glancing at Noelan, he added, “please,” before he walked off in a huff.

“Who’s your friend, Gene?”

“Oh that guy? Think he’s some kind of local banker or something,” said Eugene with a smile.

* * *

“Charlotte? Sweet girl,” said a waitress at some diner. “Always covered my shifts when I needed it. Though she hasn’t shown up for work these past few days, even though she was on the schedule...”

Noelan started unwrapping a plum candy. “Something’s not right,” he said. “Everyone keeps mentioning Falkner. And she’s not the first person who’s disappeared after dealing with him.” He popped the candy into his mouth for some sweetness.

“I don’t know, Ro. Charlotte’s got a knack for finding trouble, but…”

A child’s wail interrupted through their conversation. A little girl stood beneath a maple tree, tears on her face as she pointed upward. There, about fifteen feet up, an orange cat trembled on a branch, meowing pitifully.

“Oh wow!” Eugene’s face lit up. “I’ve always wanted to save a cat from a tree.”

Noelan rolled his eyes. “This is the most cliché thing I’ve ever seen.”

But Eugene was already shrugging off his jacket. He approached the tree and searched for a path up among the branches. With a smile on his lips and a plan on his mind, he reached for the first branch, testing, testing, testing the weight until finally he pulled himself up and began to climb.

“Here, kitty, kitty... That’s it. Just stay right there...”

The orange cat’s tail twitched once, twice, and tucked in its chin, but didn’t bolt. Eugene scooped it up and cradled it against his chest. The descent was trickier, but he managed it without incident.

“There you go, little miss,” he said, presenting the cat to its owner. The little girl’s face gleamed with joy as she hugged her pet.

“Thank you, mister!” She smiled up at him before scampering off with that cat tight in her arms.

Eugene picked his jacket off the ground. “Well, that’s one thing crossed off my bucket list.”

Noelan shook his head but couldn’t quite hide his smile. “I think we’ve done enough investigating for today.”

“Yeah.” Eugene checked his watch. “I should get going anyway. Need to practice for tonight.” He clapped Noelan on the shoulder. “Thanks for helping me look for Charlotte. Even if we didn’t find her, it means a lot.”

“Of course.”

Eugene started walking, then turned back grinning. “Oh, don’t forget! Town square at eight!”

“Don’t worry,” Noelan called after him, “I won’t forget.”

Eugene walked down that road, turning every now and then to look back at Noelan. Eugene was giving his third exaggerated wave goodbye when something more important caught Noelan’s attention.

Down a side street, past storefront windows where festival bunting was being hung, he spotted the goons from the bar, Mr Bird’s Nest and the Booty Hunter, heading toward a quieter part of Pine Ridge.

They had to be up to no good. And Noelan, because of who he was, knew there was no real choice.

The candy’s sweetness turned bitter. He swallowed it, but the bitterness remained.

Noelan glanced once more in the direction Eugene had gone, then turned and headed straight for the danger.

Noelan kept his distance as he followed the men. Opposite the late afternoon sun, cold shadows crept onto the road. A lone balloon bobbed against a fence, its string strangled in thorns.

The men turned into an alley between two storefronts. Noelan pressed himself against the wall, close enough to hear but out of sight.

“You’re gonna be missing out tonight,” the Booty Hunter said, lighting a cigarette. “Falkner’s chef is bringing in lobster. Lobster! When’s the last time you had that?”

Mr. Bird’s-Nest scoffed. “Keep your fancy fish. Festival’s got Greg’s barbecue stand; best ribs in three counties. That's the real McCoy.”

“Yeah? Well, I’ll be…”

Noelan heard a loud clatter against pavement, followed by a child’s yelp. He turned to see a kid crouched beside an overturned bike near the curb, sobbing, sniffling, and holding onto the bike like it had just died.

“Shh,” Noelan shushed, straining to hear the conversation.

The boy looked up, startled, then went right back to crying.

“Would you be quiet?” Noelan said to the kid.

The boy hiccupped. “But my bike…”

“Shh.”

The boy’s mouth snapped shut. Distraught and now confused, he tilted his head at the strange man who kept shushing him.

“...and get this, even Travis confirmed he’s coming. Man of the hour, Falkner called him.”

Noelan covered his mouth. Travis? As in Chief of Police Travis? At a party thrown by the man he was supposed to be investigating?

The goons dragged the conversation deeper into the alleyway. Noelan knew pursuit meant discovery, so he just stood by that corner, thinking through the implications until some boy’s muffled sob broke his concentration.

Noelan looked down.

The boy was standing beside him, looking all serious with both hands covering his mouth. He glanced from the bike to Noelan, then sobbed again.

“Alright, kid, what’s wrong with the bike?” Noelan asked.

“It’s broken.”

Noelan knelt by the bike to inspect it. “It’s just the chain. This is what you were crying about? You’re like that boy who cried…” He paused and smirked.

“Wolf?” The boy guessed.

“No, you didn’t cry wolf. You just cried.”

The boy glared up at Noelan with his mouth wide open.

“Now look here, it’s easy to fix.”

The kid begrudgingly watched Noelan demonstrate how to reset the chain.

“See how the chain slipped off? Next time this happens, you turn the pedal like this... no, like this.” The chain slipped back into place. “There. Now you won’t need to cry about it and bother people who are trying to work.”

“Thanks... I think,” the kid said while testing the pedals.

Noelan stood, brushing off his hands. “Scram.”

As the boy pedaled away, Noelan leaned against that brick wall. He looked up toward the hill where the crime lord’s mansion waited, hiding among those pines, overlooking Pine Ridge.

“Falkner, the mayor, and now the chief of police, huh?” he whispered to himself.

A warrant would take days, maybe weeks. Dilly-dally and the evidence would be long gone.

The streets were getting louder with the hammering and sawing and hollering of festival prep.

He needed to infiltrate that party tonight to find proof of whatever was going on between the crime lord, the chief of police, and the mayor.

Noelan reached into his pocket for a plum candy, then ate it. Sweet, then bitter; bitter as always. He crumpled the wrapper and tucked it away in another pocket.

Some promises, he decided, would have to be broken.

Chapter 7

The top of the pine tree tilted a little, just enough to feel. Noelan kept one hand loose on the branch above while he found his balance.

A brisk breeze messed with his hair and nipped his face. Rather than being uncomfortable, it gave him soothing clarity. He settled into a more comfortable position and looked at the reason he was there.

The crime lord’s mansion.

Noelan twisted to get his spyglass but caught sight of the fun he’d be skipping for tonight. The festival had the whole town lit up. From afar, it just looked like a bunch of blurry lights. The lake reflected those lights and made them twinkle, but they didn’t look anything like the stars in the sky.

Noelan ate a plum candy and tucked the wrapper away in his coat. The sweetness reminded him of Eugene and their planned meeting. It wasn’t ideal to miss it, but this was serious. People were in danger.

Noelan set the mansion in front of him and raised the spyglass to his eye.

Guards circled the grounds with clockwork regularity, following their well-lit paths like pieces on a chess board. Looking at his watch, he timed their patterns. Passing pawns would be easy.

The other game on the board, that hedge maze, would be avoided completely, but he took a few minutes to solve it in his head anyway.

The wall, too, posed no real challenge; with proper timing, he could scale it without being spotted. But those dogs...

Dog handlers traced strategic routes with their Dobermans. He’d encountered their breed before: devil dogs. Trained killers with a reputation for ferocity. If the dogs caught him, a quick death would be the best outcome. That’s what they were bred and trained for, after all.

The candy’s bitterness kicked in. Noelan pushed the taste from his mind and stowed his spyglass.

Leaning back against the trunk, he pulled out a dog whistle and spun it around his finger. The whistle would attract the dogs, but how could that possibly help?

He considered and walked through different infiltration methods; each plan fell apart because of those damn dogs.

Noelan quit fidgeting with the whistle and he sat up straighter. He stared at the whistle, then back at the mansion grounds.

If he timed it right... if he used their training against them...

He gave a knowing nod. Yes, that should work.

* * *

Noelan pressed himself against the cold wall. On the other side, boots crunched gravel: two sets, steady, getting closer… then farther. He checked his watch. Just like when he timed them from his little treehouse above, every fourteen minutes the patrols left a thirty-second gap. Confirmed three times now. There were two minutes and fifty seconds until the next one.

He crept along the wall, two hundred feet from his entry point, each step placed like the next level on a house of cards. Then he raised the dog whistle to his lips and blew. Silence... to humans. But from the grounds? Every dog on the property lost its mind.

“What’s going on?” a man yelled. “Beruhige dich!” (Calm down)

German commands. Clever. It meant the dogs wouldn’t respond to English speakers trying to control them. Noelan blew again, and again, until the mansion grounds sounded like a kennel had been tipped sideways.

“There must be something over the wall,” another man said. “Dammit Marty, get control of your dog!”

“My dog? Yours is the one about to— Fuss! FUSS!” (heel)

Noelan plucked a small stone from the ground and tossed it parallel to the wall, away from his planned infiltration point. It landed with a loud rustle in some bushes.

“I heard it! Gotta be a rabbit…” the man interrupted himself with a yelp just as the dogs bolted towards the sound. “Stop! Hey! No, Zurück! Hierher!” (Back! This way!)

Amid confusion and cursing, Noelan mouthed the commands to himself while walking back to his chosen infiltration gap. The hedge maze entrance lay just beyond the wall, though he had no intention of losing himself there, not with that clear shot to the mansion right next to the maze.

He listened to the steps on the other side. The timing had to be perfect.

He went up the wall the way a man who has peculiar hobbies might do. The stonework was old and generous with its finger-grips. Stone, grip, pull, repeat. At the top he held still and counted to ten. Stop. Wait. Look. Nothing... nothing but dogs still losing their minds and German impersonators losing their patience.

He jumped down to the pretty lawn.

The dogs wouldn’t be a problem anymore. The coast was clear. A straight shot to the mansion. Simple enough to…

Ruff!

One bark. Close. Wrong direction.

Noelan froze, then sighed. Sure, the commotion was still audible in the distance: handlers shouting commands, dogs barking at imaginary rabbits. And this bark was probably just an echo, bouncing off the mansion’s stone walls. Right? He had more pressing concerns. Thirty seconds wasn’t much time to cross the open ground to the…

Ruff!

The bark came again. Louder. Closer still.

This barking... no handler’s voice followed it. No commands to heel or stay or wienerschnitzel or whatever they were calling it.

Impossible. Wasn’t the security team too professional for that kind of oversight? They did speak German after all. Well, sorta. They wouldn’t let one of their precious imports run loose during a party with important guests, not when a single mistake could… let some bum crash the party.

That little daydream disappeared when he turned.

A Doberman. Massive. Twenty feet away and not barking anymore. He could swear the beast was grinning. All jaw and bad intentions. The thing could be sitting down and still put its paws on his shoulders. He really hoped it didn’t want to.

A string of saliva dripped from exposed fangs.

How about the hedge maze entrance? Sharp turns. Dense walls of greenery. Surely that would slow the dog. Surely.

The Doberman prepared to pounce. In that moment, Noelan knew exactly how every rabbit ever chased must’ve felt.

Chapter 8

Mansion? Or maze?

Will you run? Will you fight? Will you decide before it’s too late?

All an illusion. There’s no real choice.

Into the maze. Entrance: grass turned to gravel. Noelan slid, skidded, scraped through gravel, corner after corner. Behind him, claws scraped pebbles.

He didn’t look back.

A thorny branch bit his arm; the maze was picking sides. Noelan twisted and squeezed past another sharp branch. The path veered sharp left, sharp right, then there it was: a wooden ramp. A bridge. A way above the maze.

The stairs led up (as stairs do half the time), wide enough for two people to walk side by side. Or, in this case, wide enough for a man to sprint and a Doberman to catch up. Noelan reached the top and, for one stupid second, he felt like he’d won something.

Three seconds. Two seconds to memorize what he saw: a circular clearing, statue at the center, another bridge like this one on the far side, and…

he cursed under his breath

…a dead end.

The path he needed lay behind, but guttural growls and gouging claws disagreed.

The bridge shook. Noelan knew why but looked back anyway. The Doberman was halfway up those steps. Nowhere to squeeze through. Nowhere to take cover.

Straight ahead: a dead end.

Turn back: a dead end.

The hedge walls suddenly seemed both impossibly high and tantalizingly close. Noelan went over the railing. Not just down. Down and out...ward.

He crashed through the hedge wall. He’d take thorns over gravity every time. Still, the landing knocked the wind out of him, but he forced a roll and came up running.

He heard paws hitting gravel while he ran the paths. The turns were starting to feel like the same turn. His breath came in bursts. He kept running. Somewhere in his rattled head was the layout he’d glimpsed from above. The circular clearing with that statue wasn’t far now.

The dog’s barks grew louder. Or, more likely, the horse-dog was just closer.

Right turn. Left. Another right. The clearing had to be close. The path opened to the maze’s center: a clearing with some statue of a Greek stud whose name Noelan couldn’t care less about.

Noelan hit the statue at full sprint. He grabbed a carved cape and pulled himself up just as teeth snapped where his ankle had been. He caught onto whatever was next above him before his vision could catch up to what it was.

It never did.

Noelan couldn’t look away from the monster that wanted to kill him.

The Doberman circled the base of the statue. Noelan braced for the leap. It didn’t come. The dog wasn’t rushing. It just kept moving, nose close to the stone, pausing here, sniffing there. It stopped at one spot, crouched forward, then decided against it and continued around. It was discarding options. Ruling things out. Noelan had seen this before. Not in a dog. In professionals.

The son-of-a-bitch was doing math. Each lap around the statue left fewer possibilities, and Noelan could feel the noose tightening with every one.

“Make your move already,” Noelan said to... himself. He didn’t know how to say it in German.

Turns out, he didn’t need to.

The devil-dog’s haunches bunched. It sprang upward like a meat missile. Noelan swung sideways, barely avoiding the vicious jaws. He kicked out and caught the dog in its chest. It chirped like a stepped-on-puppy as it fought to point its feet towards the ground.

Noelan scurried up to the statue’s shoulders where, for a second, he had the luxury of another full look. The maze was a grid of wrong answers. Guards were coming. Three of them, plus another Doberman, looking for their missing mutt.

He could’ve told them where it was. It was right below him, and had started pulling itself up the statue again like it grew opposable thumbs. Noelan almost wanted to see how far it would get. Almost.

Instead, Noelan put his feet on the huge statue’s shoulders, put his hands on its head, and jumped over the nearest hedge wall.

Thorns tore at him on the way through. But thorns beat teeth, and a wall of hedge and thorns now stood between him and man’s best friend.

He knew the route now; straight past two turns, right, straight again, then take the fourth left, and boo-swasharoo right over the second bridge.

Noelan smirked on the jog towards that first right.

When he caught the tail-end of a conversation from around that corner, “...completely screwed if we don’t find Bitsy…” he stopped, dropped the smirk, and double-timed it back towards where he’d landed in the maze.

What kind of sicko names a hellhound Bitsy, anyway? And where’d those guards come from??

He slowed as he passed, heard sniffs, then three barks, then silence.

Why did Bitsy go silent!?!

Speeding back up, Noelan concocted another route to that bridge. Right before he veered left, he glanced over his shoulder.

Back where he’d landed, Bitsy was flying over the hedge wall.

He took the first right: a quick dead end into a single potted tulip.

Wrong. OK.

Then it must have been the third right.

He bolted for it. Meanwhile, his blabbermouth lungs wouldn’t shut up.

At that third right, he looked back and saw Bitsy. Maybe they meant “Itsy-Bitsy”, as in small… or a killer spider.

The bridge had to be ahead somewhere.

The path straightened, and there it was. Noelan’s relief lasted exactly as long as it took him to clock his angle of approach: the path ran under the bridge, not over it. The bridge was up there…. all the way up there…

He kept running towards it. Support beam. Wood.

Wood was the answer. Sorta.

Shoulder sheath. Knife drawn. He whizzed it into the bridge’s beam.

A guttural growl came from behind. Perhaps Bitsy was its origin story, “See? It bit the kid’s head off.”

Second knife drawn.

Bitsy lunged.

Noelan caught the beam and used the momentum to swing himself completely around it.

Bitsy flew right past him.

Noelan planted his foot on the knife stuck in the beam, and propelled himself up. He stabbed the other blade in near the top.

He pulled and hauled himself up and over the railing, rolling onto the wooden planks just as teeth clacked shut on empty air below.

He lay there a moment and just let his chest heave. Exhilarating. Then he peered over the bridge down at itsy-Bitsy down below.

Noelan laughed to himself. Even a dog bred and trained and probably hand-fed live goats couldn't jump twelve feet straight up. Sometimes, problems solve themselves.

The Doberman backed away from the bridge.

“Sorry Bitsy. This time, your math ain’t mathing,” Noelan mocked under his breath.

Bitsy stopped. Turned around. Wagged its tail.

Then Bitsy charged the support beam and ran straight up it.

Noelan leaned back like limbo.

Teeth chomped where his throat had been. Front claws caught the railing. Back legs found the edge, then the planks.

Noelan put his hand on the ground and kicked the beast back down.

Playtime was over. Noelan took off towards the mansion.

“...ridiculous maze!” A guard yelled from… somewhere. “Dammit Marty, I told you to get a hold of your dog!”

“Damn right,” Noelan whispered as he ran, silently agreeing with the guard’s assessment.

Running through the memorized paths, Noelan eased off the gas to listen for sounds of pursuit. Bitsy’s silence was more unnerving than the bark had been. No claws. No growling. Just the pounding of his heart and the pitter-patter of his own footsteps.

The exit was just ahead, two final turns away. Noelan slowed down. Time for a little sweetness, a self reward for remembering the way.

He snagged a plum candy from his pocket, unwrapped it as he turned the corner, and froze.

The exit was a left turn, ahead and just 10 feet away.

But that’s not why he froze.

Forty feet away, straight ahead along that same path, was the uber-zealatious, twice kicked, Doberman-horse-dog from hell, Bitsy.

It didn’t bark, but Bitsy wouldn’t hesitate to bite.

Noelan looked at the candy in his hand. Looked at the dog. Looked at the candy again.

“Never met a puppy that didn’t like treats,” he mumbled to himself.

He threw the candy to the dog.

Bitsy tracked the treat through the air. Up with the arc. Down with the drop. End over end it tumbled. For that one held breath, everything waited. Bitsy watched it. Noelan watched Bitsy. Even the hedges hedged their bets.

It landed: tick. A small sound. A small hope.

Bitsy stared at the grounded candy for exactly as long as it takes a dog to decide something doesn’t matter.

And ran right past it, straight at Noelan.

Noelan dashed. Not away. Forward, towards the dog, towards the danger.

Three strides.

The beast lunged for Noelan’s throat.

Noelan ducked. Dodged left.

Bitsy flew over and past. A back paw clipped Noelan’s shoulder.

Out of the maze.

Mansion. Dog behind. Balcony above.

Foot on a window ledge. Palms on a drainpipe. Up.

He reached for the balcony railing. Too far. He needed to get his feet higher, change his grip, find something between here and there. Anything for that caboose of height and had no time to get it.

From below came a growl that was hot enough to taste.

“BITSY!!!!”

The growl stopped. The paws stopped. The scythe of death… paused.

Noelan didn’t. He found the hold, pushed off, caught the railing. Over and up the railing and onto the balcony floor.

He looked down through the railing bars.

Bitsy stood at the base of the wall, staring up at him. Then back towards the voice from within the maze. Snout up. Back. Up. Back.

A small growl. Hind limbs tensed.

“BITSY! Hierher, jetzt!” (Get over here, now!) came another yell from beyond the hedge walls.

A whimper of a bark eked out.

Then Bitsy tucked its tail and trotted back into the maze.

The darkness beyond those French doors tasted like secrets and smelled like Jabberwocky.

And so... Noelan decided to take in a big whiff.

Chapter 9

Darkness.

Noelan let his breathing slow while his eyes adjusted. He was exactly where he’d planned to be during his earlier surveillance. Never doubted it for a second.

The yellow light from beneath the door across the room made dark silhouettes out of the decor. A sofa. He hit his shin on what felt like the corner of a table. The sound of glass and sloshing liquid.

A tumbler?

He shuffled over and pawed for it again until he felt glass. Short and heavy glass. Room temperature. Noelan whiffed it. Whiskey. He placed it back down, crept to the amber seeping out from under the door, and tuned his ears to the other side.

He held his breath, heard nothing, then quietly opened the door.

The light hit him before his eyes could catch up, but he could tell what lay beyond before he could fully see. It was a hallway. Noelan closed the door.

He couldn’t leave the room. Not like this. He needed to blend in.

So, he waited.

Footsteps approached. Too much stomp, not enough hurry. Not a server. A guard.

Noelan unwrapped a plum candy once the guard had passed. Standing there in the dark with the sweet in his mouth, he fidgeted with the wrapper and with an idea.

When a server passed by some trash in this immaculate mansion, they’d surely stop to pick it up. And when they did, Noelan would pick up his disguise.

The candy turned bitter and he swallowed it.

He cracked open the door to check both ways. Still empty. Still quiet. Noelan took a couple of soft steps down the clean corridor and placed the candy wrapper on the Persian hall-rug.

Heading back, he saw it, and stopped completely.

Near the entrance to the dark room, on that fancy Persian carpet, were two dark stains. Red drops on a red rug. He needed to get out of the hallway anyway before anyone came by.

Noelan went back into the room, but before he closed the door, light refracted through the tumbler; the whiskey inside was pale, diluted with the ghost of an ice cube. It had been waiting much longer than he had.

The door met the frame and took the light with it.

Darkness and waiting; the hand and glove they never publish in spy recruitment brochures.

Now and then, he’d hear a muffled little nothing from the party, a shout or a loud laugh, but no footsteps.

Then, after what felt like hours later, but was probably minutes, footsteps approached from his left. Light. Quick. Waiter-like. They reached the spot where the wrapper sat and kept right on going. Not even a stutter in the stride.

Slob.

Noelan opened the door to check on the wrapper. Yup, still there. Didn’t blow away or nothing. He grabbed every single wrapper from his pocket, must have been twelve or so, and scattered them around on the rug.

Now they can’t miss it.

He hurried back to the room, turned the knob before pulling the door shut, and held it quiet. Just Noelan and the dark, waiting together again.

Footsteps. Swift ones. That stopped at bait (or seemed to).

Ready to pounce, he eased the door open just enough to see.

There, squatting on the Persian rug, a server plucked candy wrappers off the floor like a man defusing tiny bombs. One by one. Careful. Thorough. Finally, a man who cares about a job done right.

Too bad for him, he was…. scratch that. Too bad for Noelan, the man was five foot nothing. A hundred and nothing.

A gingerbread man, in a gingerbread sized server’s outfit.

He closed the door and leaned his head on the wall beside the door, and fought like hell to not audibly sigh.

Out the door, he heard, “Hey man, what’ve you got there?”

“Would you look at this mess? Someone dropped… there must be fifteen candy wrappers here!”

It was ten.

“What? What a slob…”

Gingerbread Man and the Muffin Man. Both just chatting it up there, like lickety and split on a coffee break, instead of picking up the litter and leaving.

* * *

Eventually, Noelan got a server outfit (that fit) with a full tray of champagne flutes. And an unconscious man was left tied up on the floor next to the whiskey.

In his new pockets, he managed to cram in his gloves, wallet, and lock picks. His gun was holstered under the jacket.

The kitchen wasn’t hard to find. They weren’t trying to hide it. It was like a game of hot and cold: the closer he got, the stronger the aroma of ostentatious cuisine.

Noelan passed through the swinging doors and adopted the manner of someone who owned the place…. had hired him to be a waiter.

Servers weaved between prep stations, line cooks barked orders, and somewhere a pot was threatening to boil over. The yelling of the head chef drowned the general clatter of pots and pans.

“No, no, nooo! My god, what is this?” The head chef snatched the plate from the pass, holding it aloft like evidence in a trial. His free hand flailed in exasperation as he turned to face a nervous-looking sous chef. “This quail! It’s a shriveled husk! Did it expire from boredom waiting to be served? Are we catering the crème de la crème of Pine Ridge or running a hospice for cancer birds?!?” He thrust the plate into the hands of the now distraught-looking sous chef, who nearly fumbled it. “Do it again!”

“Yes, chef.” The sous chef scurried away.

“And you!” The chef whirled toward a line cook. “The mayor’s pie must be treated like royalty! A masterpiece deserves a throne, not this...” he pointed at the display stand with disgust, “this peasant’s pedestal!”

Noelan found the pie in question. Golden brown crust, latticed beautifully, concealing what looked like... raspberry? No, blackberry filling. He nodded in approval.

“Hurry up with those champagne flutes!” someone called out to Noelan. “The guests in the ballroom are getting testy!”

Noelan pushed through the swinging doors. The scent of roasted meat and burnt sugar got choked out by cigar smoke and too much perfume.

Beneath vaulted ceilings, the local big-wigs mingled amidst crystal and candlelight, their conversations spiked with guffaws, champagne clinks, and the glint of jewels. Noelan moved among them with everyday invisibility; flutes were embezzled away from his tray while conversations ebbed and flowed around him.

“...and her work with the children’s hospital has been remarkable,” a portly businessman was saying to his prospects. “Not to mention the way she handled the budget surplus. Who knew a woman could have such a head for numbers?”

“She’s got quite the head for business too,” another man added, accepting a flute from Noelan’s tray with barely a glance. “The town’s finances haven’t been this solid in years. Though I must say, her pies alone would get my vote!”

Noelan drifted onward, catching fragments of similar praise as he circulated. The mayor had quite the fan club among Pine Ridge’s businessmen. But also, near a gilt-framed mirror, a group of women huddled together.

“…barely finished arranging the flowers for his funeral before she was arranging campaign meetings,” one woman murmured, the pearls gleaming at her throat bracing to be clutched.

“Well, what was she supposed to do?” another replied. “Let the town fall apart? Harvey was a fine mayor, God rest his soul, but that hunting accident…”

“‘Accident,’” a third woman repeated with meaningful emphasis. “Rather convenient timing, wasn’t it? Right after the election…”

Sometimes people feel when they are being watched.

“Margaret!”

Noelan perked up before quickly remembering his poker face.

Margaret seemed to sense Noelan was watching, and stared right at him. Then she grinned and continued talking to the group, “Well, I’m just saying what everyone’s thinking. And now she’s running again...”

“It’s about time a man stepped up,” the first woman declared. “This town needs proper leadership, not a woman playing a man’s role.”

“You know, I heard Gill’s running this time... Might give the mayor a run for her money,” Margaret said, idly swirling her champagne.

Noelan passed another group gathered near a marble column. A woman in green was gesturing enthusiastically to her rapt audience. “The secret is in the Ghirardelli chocolate. Imported, of course. And the way the ganache settles between the layers...” She sighed contentedly. “Wouldn’t you say it’s absolute perfection, Vera?”

Her maid, a woman holding a half-empty plate, nodded quickly. “Oh yes, madam. It’s... it shall be exquisite. A masterpiece; truly!”

“You haven’t tried it?” One of the guests asked, surprised.

“Well, no.” The woman laughed slightly. “But Vera has excellent taste. Don’t you, dear?”

“Yes, madam. I wish I could taste such excellence.”

“No, that’s not... anyway, it will certainly outshine anything else served.” She cast a glance toward the kitchen. “Even our dear mayor’s famous pie.”

Then, from behind, Noelan heard, “...and you certainly throw the best parties,” followed by laughter. He turned to see where it came from.

Three groups away, there he stood. A big, beautiful monster of a man.

Forty or so. Chiseled from marble like some Hellenic Hero holding court. When he smiled at a guest’s joke, it reached his eyes with genuine warmth. And the circle of minions and fawners wasn’t faking it either. The man was magnetic, charming.

Noelan moved closer.

“The streets have never been cleaner,” a man was saying. “Whatever you’re doing with that waste management contract, keep doing it!”

“We all must do our part to keep Pine Ridge beautiful,” agreed another guy in the circle, so scrawny he could have been the Gingerbread Man’s father.

A man with a green silk tie and mechanic’s hands approached the crime lord. Noelan busied himself nearby, adjusting glasses that needed no adjustment.

“Mr. Falkner,” the man said in a low voice. “I wanted to thank you for handling that situation with David. I didn’t think anyone could make him see reason, but whatever you did...”

But he wasn’t talking to the Greek God. He was addressing the circus dwarf next to him.

“Sometimes people need the right motivation to do the right thing,” the short guy replied kindly. “I trust he won’t be causing any more problems?”

That. THAT? is the crime lord? Small town small problems.

“None at all. It’s good to know someone’s willing to put so much work into taking out the trash in this town,” the man said with a chuckle. “The mayor made the right choice supporting your efforts to keep Pine Ridge clean.”

“We all want what’s best for our community,” Falkner said. “Some people just need education to understand their role in it.”

Another gentleman in an immaculate suit appeared at Falkner’s shoulder. “Speaking of community,” he cut in, “I hear your daughter’s piano recital was quite the success, Mr. Miller. Beethoven, wasn’t it?”

The conversation swam to safer waters, but Noelan had heard enough to speculate; plus he was becoming a bit much of a suspicious fixture, anyway.

And so, Noelan continued his rounds, watching immaculate-suit-man’s busy action. He’d be at Falkner’s shoulder during business discussions but melted away for social chatter, only to reappear whenever conversations threatened to become interesting. A slight eye movement from Falkner was all it took… sometimes not even that.

Noelan had him pegged by the third intervention. If Falkner wasn’t going to let anything else slip, it was time to see what others might have to say.

He served a glass to someone near a window, drawn to the garden party below. More hedges but, mercifully, no Dobermans. String lights twinkled, kids danced to a live band, couples strolled arm-in-arm through paths, but Noelan’s attention got snagged by the woman smiling her way through conversations.

Mayor Coolidge.

Same face from the election posters. Wholesome as Blackberry Pie.

Eavesdropping on the crime lord was going nowhere. Anytime the conversation got close to incriminating, immaculate-suit-man would pop in and distract, help steer the conversation, or fix it.

“Mr. Fixit,” Noealn mouthed to himself.

Noelan meant to take a quick glance, but when he turned, Mr. Fixit was already looking at him.

For a fraction of a second, neither looked away. Then, Noelan casually turned back to the champagne service. There had been some recognition in that sharp stare. He smiled slightly. That was not good.

He couldn’t look directly again. Instead, Noelan tilted a champagne flute, trying to catch Mr. Fixit’s reflection in the curved glass. It was futile; the distorted glass showed only smears of color and light.

Noelan set the flute down on his tray and walked toward some useless-looking console table standing against a wall. A clean, beautiful gilt-framed mirror hung right above it. It was the perfect rearview mirror. He stole a glimpse into it.

Mr. Fixit had moved closer to the center of the room... and closer to where Noelan stood.

From afar, he seemed focused on some simple conversation, just smiling and cracking easy jokes. But Noelan noticed that he wasn’t even with his boss anymore. And that he had just glanced at him again.

Noelan lifted his tray and headed for the kitchen.

It could be an overread. Mr. Fixit could’ve just needed a little champagne to loosen up.

But maybe he already knew.

Either way, Noelan was on his way to find out.

Chapter 10

Noelan wove through the mansion’s corridors, stopping under the cover of swapping glasses and collecting empties. He’d make sudden turns, double back through rooms, or adjust items for a little too long at a service station, all while snatching reflections in mirrors and windows.

Somewhere between two doorways the air committed a small betrayal. A coal-tar sharpness, camphorous, astringent, a vapor that prickled the top of his sinuses like an uninvited guest who knew exactly where everything was, then took off before he could get its name, the way evidence disappears in a small town. Sweet the way old storage is sweet. Noelan gave it none of his attention. Not yet.

The first two times he circled back near Falkner, Mr. Fixit was nowhere to be seen. The third time, he caught a glimpse in a mirror. Coincidence? Perhaps. Part of a fixer’s job is being everywhere at once.

Regardless, it was too much heat. The game wasn’t worth the candle.

Noelan Houdini’d from the ballroom to go explore, to find out what else the mansion might be hiding up its sleeves.

It didn’t take long at all. The crooked mansion had many sleeves.

Noelan had been staring down a wall at the end of a corridor that couldn’t keep its story straight.

The wood on this stretch sat half a shade too light, the way a lie sits when it sounds good but the face don’t quite add up.

It was a small thing… the kind of small thing that can crack cases.

First he interrogated the wall trim with the little medallion in the middle by giving it a light kick. Its alibi was airtight.

Then he turned to the next suspect: the decorative lamp. He scoped his surroundings before giving it a pull. Cold trail.

The last suspect was the wall itself. The line the wall was running was hinky. A con. One of the wood panels looked like it was planted there; the grooves around it hid a deeper meaning.

He glanced over his shoulder.

No witnesses.

Noelan pressed the panel for answers. The panel gave in.

A section of the wall popped out of its frame. Noelan pushed the door and peered into the darkness of the secret passage. He went in quick and closed the door.

On this side, the mansion stopped pretending.

The volume on the party’s hum turned down to one. Old, but not dusty. Dark, but not pitch black. The cracks that let in light did so on purpose.

Noelan crept through the hush of a space built to keep secrets, and kept them well, until he hit an intersection.

Right or left?

“Two roads diverged in a yellow… Stop that,” Noelan said, talking to himself again.

Not much of a difference. He chose left.

He walked, and walked, and then there was a door. He didn’t hear anyone on the other side. Noelan opened it a tad bit to peek out. Just an empty secondary kitchen, but there was a basket of blackberries on the counter.

Noelan didn’t linger. He shut the secret door and went back to the intersection. This time, he took the right path. At the end, he flicked a lever and the door popped open.

Out. Another room. Suspicious wall. Secret passage. Out again. Fake painting. In again. So many passages. What’s with all the mazes?

Noelan had mapped out a web of hidden routes through the mansion. The latest passage ended at a cobblestone wall. Fortunately, no minotaur. But there was a lever begging to be pulled.

The door opened to a staircase landing. He walked out and admired how well this passage was hidden from the outside, when the sound of footsteps stopped him like a red light after a yellow that wasn’t yellow for long enough.

A waiter with ears that stuck out was descending the stairs toward a wine cellar, back exposed to Noelan. Not quite Dumbo, but…

Noelan quickly pushed the stone wall shut, but it was too loud.

Dumbo turned back sharply and almost tripped down the steps.

“When did you get behind….!!! Uhh….” the waiter tried to say, but when it didn’t work, he resorted to laughing while looking up the steps he’d just walked. “What??”

Noelan looked at him like he was the crazy one and walked past, visibly rolling his eyes.

He opened the door and took a couple of steps into a wine cellar, looking around like a husband on a home buyer’s mansion tour.

The place was built funny. It was too square to be a corridor and too long to be a room, like the architectural equivalent of a shrug. Bottles slept in wooden racks on both walls. Crates sat between the rows like afterthoughts, while a few historic bottles got display shelves all their own.

At the opposite end of the wine cellar, a second set of double doors became Noelan’s new objective.

Dumbo squeezed past him with a quiet “Excuse me.”

Noelan quit loitering near the door and started down the wine cellar.

He didn’t get far.

Three people were in the room: Dumbo (the waiter from the stairs), another server who looked like he’d rather be anywhere else on Earth, maybe even the bottom of Pine Ridge Lake, and the guy next to him.

Mr. Fixit.

Noelan scurried over beside Dumbo, who was searching for the right bottle.

“Forty-two... forty-two...” the man was muttering.

Noelan picked up a bottle and looked at the label, but didn’t really bother reading it. He was about to put it back when he noticed Dumbo staring at him. Noelan peeked past him to see if Mr. Fixit had noticed him. He hadn’t.

Noelan glared at Dumbo as if to say, “The hell you looking at? Get back to work.”

The guy quickly turned away, but went right back to slacking off when the door on the far end of the cellar opened.

In walked three more servers.

One had a wine bottle in a choke-hold, another dragged a crate to the corner with a scrape that made Noelan wince and bite his tongue, and the last one just trailed behind them, giggling at anything and smiling for nothing.

They popped a squat around the crate and passed the open bottle. Then the dice rolled out.

Dumbo glanced at Noelan for a split second then clamped a hand over his mouth. Noelan, who was already trying to keep quiet, looked up at the ceiling and breathed in.

Did these clowns really not see Mr. Fixit standing right there?

“What exactly is going on here?” Mr. Fixit reproached.

One server, either braver or drunker than his companions, took a deliberate swig. “What guests shouldn’t be seeing, if you catch my drift.”

“Sir, we’re sorry,” another cut in quickly, shooting his friend a warning look. “We were just…”

“Fork it over, nosebleeds.” Mr. Fixit held out his hand for the bottle. When the guy took too long, he snatched it from him. He started to ask, “Have you seen…” but trailed off at the sight of those stupid smiles and dumb looks.

“Hit the bricks,” he ordered instead.

“Yes, sir! Sorry about that, sir!” They scattered, suddenly very diligent in their duties. “Was it the ’32 Bordeaux he wanted? Or the ’34?”

“The ’25, you maroon.”

“I thought it was champagne...”

Mr. Fixit stood for a moment, weighing the half-empty bottle in his hand with a frown before walking past Noelan and up the stairs. The man was probably heading back to the party.

Noelan watched the servers pretend to play work.

Eventually, one cracked, whispering, “He gone yet?”

“Dunno. Jake, go check.”

Jake crept toward the exit Mr. Fixit took and peered up the stairs like a kid playing Cloak and Dagger. The real spy watched this with a smirk. Not a bad precaution, really. Jake ran back to the others. “Coast is clear. Bet you anything he’s drinking the rest of that bottle himself.”

The wine-brave server fixed his bow tie. “We should grab something nice for the chef.”

“That’s not the vintage he asked for,” one of the servers added kindly.

“That’s the point. Once he opens it...” The wine-brave server’s grin said the rest.

Noelan walked past the clowns and took the exit opposite the stairs Mr. Fixit had taken.

He headed toward band music when, halfway down another hallway, he heard a faint sob. Then another. Following them led to double French doors with a plaque hanging from the doorknobs that read “DO NOT ENTER”.

After a quick look to each side, he (obviously) nudged open the door.

Under the glass ceiling, under the moon, and under the light from a far away party, little tea tables with their charming little chairs waited for conversations among pretty plants and fugitive flowers.

But he hadn’t come for the ambiance; he’d come for the mini-mystery of the sobs.

The peaceful sounds of choking, gurgling water came from a fountain built around a great stone lion. The lion’s face was carved into a furious snarl, but the choice to have the water drool from its mouth might lead an art critic to opine that it wasn’t angry, but hungry. Or perhaps…

And there, sitting by the hungry lion, he found the answer to what had led him here.

She looked up at the sound of his footsteps but didn’t bother wiping her tears. “You’re not supposed to be here.”

“Neither of us are,” Noelan said softly.

A strand of hair had come loose from her updo. Without thinking, he reached out and tucked it behind her ear. She didn’t pull away, just looked at him.

“I’m Tori. You one of the servers?” she asked rhetorically.

“No.” He smiled. “I’m a secret agent.”

She stared for a moment then played along. “You’re a spy? Let me guess, are you here because of the disappearances?”

“Yeah. How did you know?” Noelan (jokingly) admitted. Then he added, “Are you a detective?”

She put on a serious face, which was already cracking, and said, “Yes, I am a detective.”

They both broke out laughing.

When it died down, she wiped her eyes and sighed. “You know, for a second, I thought you were... never mind.”

Noelan let the silence sit for a bit, then, casual as a coin flip, asked, “Do I look familiar to you?”

“Hmm…” She didn’t rush to reply, just thought for a moment, then ignored the question. “Mr. Secret Agent, I thought you’d be more interested in Marcus.”

“Marcus, huh? Who’s that? A friend of Falkner’s?”

“No, more like a business associate. He’s from out of town, but he always drops by for parties and meetings. Never misses these events. You know? And yet…”

“When did you last see him?”

“About three weeks ago? He was… It’s strange. He was so jazzed about tonight’s party.” She looked away and covered her mouth. “Now that I say it like this, he might really be missing. I thought maybe the deal with Irving had gone bad, but...”

She stopped talking and contemplated Noelan.

Silence decorated by running water hanged in the air.

The plants looked pretty. The lion looked hungry. The floor looked…. wrong. It was the kind of wrong he’d seen before. Right next to the lion fountain. He approached the suspicious lion. He knew it had something to hide.

Tori looked at Noelan, who was now standing on the ledge of the fountain she’d been sitting on, and quietly asked, “What’s up?”

“I think…. There!” One of the lion’s claws was a switch. He flicked it to the side and turned toward the part of the floor that had just popped out of the ground. “Well, would you look at that?”

Tori was already on her feet. “Is that a… secret passage!?”

Noelan jumped down and lightly kicked it open.

“Care to find out where it leads?” He offered his palm. “It’s got to be more fun than pouting in a plant room.”

“As a detective, I’m obligated to investigate.” She took his hand. “Let’s go now, Mr. Secret Agent.”

Chapter 11

“There are passages like this all through the mansion,” Noelan quietly explained as they walked. “Old servant corridors, mostly. Though this one feels different.”

“Where do you think it leads?” Tori’s whisper carried equal parts excitement and conspiracy.

“Let’s find out.”

When the passage ended at another wall, Noelan found another switch, and the secret door swung outward… into empty air. They were behind a massive painting. Beyond it lay what appeared to be a private sitting room, all dark wood and rich.

Noelan was helping Tori down when he heard footsteps from the hallway outside. He pulled her back into the passage and pulled the painting-door shut.

He pressed a finger to his lips and nodded towards the sound.

“Mr. Secret Agent,” she whispered dramatically, “are you trying to be romantic?”

Before Noelan could respond, the door opened. From behind the painting, they could hear two people enter the room.

“I need to sit down,” a woman announced. Noelan recognized that voice. It was the woman from earlier (the one with the maid). “That horrible pie... am I to be defeated again?”

“No, madam.” The maid’s voice was firm.

“You really think so?” Hope crept into the woman’s tone. “You’d bet on me winning?”

“Honestly?”

“Yes, tell me!”

“I think...” A delicate pause. “You’ll definitely win second place, madam.”

In the passage, Noelan bit back a laugh. Tori pressed her face against his shoulder to muffle her own giggles.

A dramatic sigh filled the room. “Not good enough. That mayor and her perfect pies... something simply must be done.”

“The pie could... disappear before the tasting,” the maid suggested carefully.

“Vera!” Rustling fabric suggested the woman had sat up. “That would be absolutely terrible.” She then paused. “Do you think you can manage it?”

“It would be no problem, madam. I got you.”

“You’re a treasure,” the woman said. “Now, we should return before we’re missed.”

The door opened then closed. They heard their footsteps stomp away until it was quiet. Noelan and Tori waited a moment longer before bursting out laughing.

Noelan helped Tori down from the passage. She smoothed her dress, still fighting back laughter.

“Quite the scandal,” he said, settling into one of the armchairs. “Though I have to say, that pie did look impressive when I passed through the kitchen. All golden-brown and perfect.”

She perched on the arm of his chair. “Do mysterious secret agents usually have a sweet tooth?”

“Cyanide teeth, actually,” Noelan said, pointing at his jaw with a grin.

Tori giggled, then caught herself and leaned in like she was going to spill state secrets.

“Tonight’s dessert tasting is quite the event,” she whispered. “Every year the mayor’s blackberry pie goes up against Marion’s chocolate cake. It’s the talk of…”

Footsteps in the hallway interrupted her.

A male ‘waiter’ being found alone with a female guest in a private room would raise exactly the wrong kind of attention. Noelan glanced at the painting, but there wasn’t time. He hid behind the red curtain near the window instead.

“Miss Tori?” The door opened and a server’s head popped in. “Miss... Finally, I found you. Your father’s looking for you. Something about introducing you to... Mc... Mckal? Cali? I can’t remember his name. It’s someone’s son.”

“Oh, good lord,” Tori sighed. “Tell him I’ll be there in a moment.” She glanced at Noelan. He quietly shook his head, then she looked back to the server. “You wouldn’t happen to have a pencil and paper, would you?”

The server patted his pockets until he found a small notepad and stub of pencil. Tori wrote something quickly, tore off the page, and handed the supplies back. “Thank you.”

The server nodded and withdrew. Tori waited until he was gone before closing the door and calling out, “You can come out now, Romeo.”

Noelan emerged from behind the curtain. “Romeo, hm?”

“Well, we did just spend time hiding from my father’s matchmaking attempts.”

Noelan laughed.

“Thanks for the fun little escapade,” she said, then her smile dimmed a bit. “I have to go before they send another search party.”

“Finally.”

“Hey!” she said with a playful swat at his chest. She felt something in the pocket and fished out one of his plum candies.

“I’ll take this,” she said, “as a barter for what I left for you in there.”

Noelan reached into his pocket and found a folded piece of paper with a phone number. “Better than two chickens and a loaf of bread.”

“Call me sometime. Maybe we can have another adventure,” she said with a smirk.

Then she was gone.

Noelan stood there for a moment, one hand unconsciously covering the smile he was too busy for… while he read the number again. He folded the paper and tucked it away.

His thoughts wandered from Tori to the conversation they had overheard. All this pie theft and sneaking around reminded him of another investigation, years ago. He could almost hear Eugene’s laughter, telling that story about the elephant and the windowsill pies. Though this time, instead of an elephant, the culprit was a maid. He wondered how she was going to pull it off; the mayor’s pie laid in plain sight right there in the kitchen.

Noelan stopped smiling.

“The mayor…”

He needed to see what she was up to… and how she might be wrapped up in the murders.

* * *

Petit fours. A whole tray of them, stacked and glazed and looking like tiny, little, bite sized gifts. One of them even had a candied strawberry on top with some gold leaves sprinkled on.

It looked wonderful…

“Oh, these look wonderful! Don, my dear, have you tried one yet?” a fancy dressed woman said.

…and it was the first to be snatched away.

Two guests had found him before he’d found anyone worth eavesdropping on. The man (Don, apparently) was holding a cigar and the woman was holding onto his arm… and wearing his coat on her shoulders.

She showed Don the petit four, then took a bite.

“Mmm, this is to die for,” she said while holding it up to the garden lights at an angle that made it glimmer. Noelan looked away for a split second then snapped back to it when she took another one. She ate it in one bite.

“I mean these are really good,” the woman said after taking and eating another one

Noelan gulped down a sigh.

Then she took ANOTHER one and offered it to Don.

“You have to try this,” she said.

“Come on, dear, you know I don’t eat sweets.”

“Just try one bite.”

He begrudgingly took the tiniest bite possible then looked at her in surprise.

“Mm! Wow, you’re right. This is fantastic.”

It can’t be that good.

“This is the best dessert I’ve ever had!”

This had to be on purpose.

“Let’s go back inside,” The woman said while pulling the coat closer to herself. “It’s so cold out here.”

“I told you not to wear that dress.”

“But it’s so pretty! I just had to wear it.”

Then the couple took three more and left.

Noelan stood there in the cold, watching them go back into the mansion, then shook his head and turned away.

He still had work to do.

Noelan took to wandering through the garden party with that emptier tray of petit fours.

There were no flowers out here. No fountains, no hungry lion statues, no secret passages…. probably…. but there was a band playing on a small stage, and though there weren’t any dancers on the dance floor, there was this one guy shouting out “Woo!” every now and then to cheer the band.

The music was solemn and boring. The trumpet tried too hard and the bass didn’t try at all. But that was all fine.

Nobody was listening anyway.

Everyone has intentions. Some came to enjoy the food. Some came to catch up with friends. Some came because of the mayor.

People would approach her and she would shake their hand and exchange pleasantries. Others would fawn around and wait for their turn to talk about what really mattered.

“...and the children’s hospital renovation is nearly complete,” a man was saying. “Your fundraising efforts have been remarkable. We truly appreciate it.”

“Pine Ridge’s children deserve the very best we can give them,” the lady mayor replied.

“And that new park by the lake,” someone else butted in. “My grandchildren adore it.”

“The real credit goes to our generous donors,” she diverted with a wave. “Pine Ridge has always had such a strong sense of community. Speaking of, how is Leslie doing?”

“Oh, he’s…”

Noelan offered the sweets on his tray to the nearby well wishers. They brought compliments to her feet like cats dragging in half-dead birds, cute and unneeded. He kept listening.

“Speaking of community projects,” a familiar woman in pearls was saying, “I heard your blackberry pie is a favorite to win tonight’s competition again.”

“You’re too kind. Marion’s chocolate cake is formidable competition. Though I admit, I am trying something new with the lattice this year.”

“You simply must tell me your secret,” the woman pleaded. “I’ve tried everything, but my crust never comes out quite so perfect.”

“Family recipe,” the mayor replied with a conspiratorial wink. “Some traditions are meant to be kept.”

Someone bumped into Noelan but didn’t say anything. It was a server. Noelan watched the server cut through the crowd toward the mayor. He was trying to walk calmly. He was not walking calmly.

When he reached her, he squeaked out an “Excuse me, Mrs…. Mrs. mayor…” and whispered something to her. The mayor murmured a word back with a confused expression.

“Yes..!!” The server’s voice escaped the whisper, like a hamster out of a chewed up box.

The pearl woman perked up so fast her necklace swung. Then she leaned in with the subtlety of a woman who had never once in her life been accused of minding her own business.

The server caught her and fixed her with a look that said everything he didn’t dare to out loud.

Noelan shook his head in disappointment, as if he wasn’t doing the exact same thing. He wasn’t; he was doing it right.

The mayor sighed through a smile, whispered something to the server, and turned to her fans, saying, “Forgive me, I need to attend to something. Please, enjoy yourselves.”

Then she followed the server into the mansion.

Noelan ditched his tray. He heard the pearl woman pulling the nearest ear toward her and whispering loudly, “Did you see the way she…” but didn’t stick around to hear any of it. He had bigger fish to fry.

Back in the mansion. Tailing the mayor. Noelan was always one turn behind.

The red carpeting swallowed his footsteps, which was useful, but it swallowed theirs too, which was not.

He tracked them with timing and the occasional peek around a corner.

Peek, pull back, count to five, peek again, go, stop, wait on the corner, peek again, and so on.

Then, from the far end of the corridor he hadn’t turned into yet, he heard the sound of doors. It wasn’t exactly the sound of a door shutting but it was obviously a door. He hoped it wasn’t the kind of door he knew it was.

Noelan rounded the corner and looked at the double doors.

The kitchen. They were headed to the kitchen.

He rubbed his brows with his knuckles.

The kitchen? It didn’t make sense. Unless... maybe that’s exactly why they chose it. Who would suspect torture in a kitchen during a party?

“No one, ’cause that would be stupid,” Noelan retorted to himself.

Then, down the hallway, coming from around the corner and heading toward the kitchen, he saw the maid from before. She was licking something from her thumb with a smirk. When she got to the door, she fixed her face, then entered the kitchen.

Noelan laughed a little under his breath and started towards the kitchen.

Two men came around the corner ahead of him, heading his way. Just security. Noelan didn’t think much of it until the distance closed enough for recognition to land like a cold hand on the back of his neck.

The Booty Hunter and the leader from the bar.

The Booty Hunter was bouncing around on his toes while punching the air and ducking invisible hooks. “...Swear if I caught that cop sneaking around, oh man. Bam! It’d be lights out for him. But if that wasn’t enough then I’d…” He shadow boxed a combination at nothing. “And you could…”

Noelan kept walking. Same pace. Same posture. The distance kept closing.

The leader chuckled. “You’d trip over your own feet hopping around like that.”

Noelan gave a slight nod as he passed.

The leader didn’t look. The Booty Hunter did. His walk hitched. Not a stop; a hitch, the kind of hitch where the legs want to keep moving but the brain pulls the handbrake.

Was that recognition in his eyes?

Noelan kept walking. He did not look back.

Behind him, one set of footsteps stopped. The other kept going a few paces before stopping too.

Silence.

Then the footsteps resumed. Quicker now. Moving away.

Noelan reached the kitchen doors and exhaled. He knew he needed to wrap things up soon.

Chapter 12

Dessert contestants gathered in tight circles, trading theories in voices just loud enough to spread. At the stoves, two line cooks had stopped pretending to work and were leaning on the counter with their chins propped on their fists.

A server near the door had come in to refill a tray of champagne flutes and was now on his third minute of doing absolutely nothing with it.

The display stand near the center of the kitchen was empty. The sweet, delicious-looking blackberry pie had vanished.

Noelan looked directly at the maid. She was looking at Marion with her lips puckered out and her brows up high. Marion nodded while smiling.

The head chef’s tantrum had been building while Noelan took this in. “Sabotage! In MY kitchen!” He paced in front of the empty stand like a prosecutor without a jury. “This is unconscionable! Unforgivable! Uncon… possible!”

The chef turned to the mayor. “Madam Mayor, I assure you we will find the culprit! To think someone would dare steal the pie that was destined to win you your sixth consecutive victory…”

He clutched the sides of his chef’s hat as if it might fly off his head from the sheer force of his rage. “Rumor was that the pigs had disappeared from Pine Ridge. Apparently, they’re not all gone yet.”

The maid stood there, biting her tongue and chewing on the jeer like it was a bone she deigned to spit back.

The mayor walked over to the empty display and shook her head, saying, “That wasn’t the pie.”

The chef stared at her. “...What?”

The maid looked just as lost.

“Yeah, that was…” The mayor pressed her lips together, breathed in, and tried again. “That was just a display piece. I haven’t made the competition pie yet; it tastes better freshly baked.”

She glanced at Marion and a laugh escaped. She coughed it down.

The kitchen staff looked like prisoners who’d just been pardoned, but Noelan noticed more than a few contestants’ smiles were fake as Bloomingdale’s Santa Claus.

The maid cringed and covered her eyes with her hand. Marion looked to the side and sighed. The chef seemed most affected; he opened and closed his mouth several times, like some confused bear.

The mayor noticed this confusion and helpfully added, “I was going to start making it soon, though.”

Noelan turned away. He heard the head chef’s fit start again while he left the kitchen: “But still! The principle of the matter! In my kitchen...”

He reached into his pocket for a plum candy. The initial sweetness hit as he considered the scene. The mayor’s connection to Falkner remained unclear. Frustrating.

As always, the bitter aftertaste kicked in. Perhaps it was time to look for more concrete evidence, something Captain Gill could use to catch the crime lord ‘the right way’.

The crime lord’s study was the logical next step. There had to be something there: documents, letters, records, maybe a pony… anything that would prove he was behind the murders.

Noelan waited until the hallway was clear, then pressed the hidden button in the wall panel. The passage opened and he stepped in. He’d mapped most of these servants’ corridors during his earlier explorations, but it still felt like navigating a maze.

The path diverged up ahead.

Right at the junction would take him towards the ballroom. Left would lead closer to the crime lord’s study. Noelan stopped when he heard voices through the wall.

“...checked the east wing again?”

“Yeah, heading to the west side now.”

Could be routine. Could be that they’re onto him. He didn’t know which, and not knowing made him careful.

He turned left and followed the passage until it ended at a hidden door. This one opened into an empty sitting room. The room had clearly fallen out of regular use, though someone still dusted occasionally.

Noelan opened the sitting room’s door and peeked into the hallway. Empty. The guard patrol would pass by in roughly two minutes if their pattern held.

He slowly got out of the room and began walking toward the painting at the end of the corridor.

As he walked, he felt a prickling sensation on his back.

Noelan turned around.

He didn’t see anyone.

He stared on for a moment longer, then picked up the pace.

The study was a floor up, so getting there meant either risking the main staircase or finding another secret passage. He preferred the latter.

Thunk.

He stopped.

Thunk. Thunk.

He looked behind him.

There was nothing there, but he still heard something.

Thunk. Thunk.

Opposite direction.

Thunk. Thunk. Thunk.

Footsteps. Fast. Coming closer. From around the corner.

Noelan quietly stepped back while keeping his focus at the end of the corridor.

He found a dark alcove and backed into it. Not blinking. Not breathing.

Shoes ran toward him. He heard them come and saw them go… just a server.

Noelan waited until he was alone, then skulked back to the painting. He reached for the hidden switch, but then did a double take at what was there on the canvas.

It was Napoleon. Not on the battlefield, not astride a horse. Just a man by a campfire. Late at night. After a campaign. Entirely alone. The uniform still on but unbuttoned. Maybe he’s staring at the flames. Maybe beyond, at something the painter couldn’t show. There's a weariness to him. A sadness.

Noelan pressed the hidden switch and the painting popped open.

The stairs inside the passage were steep and loud. Every step had a complaint. Noelan climbed them the way you cross a frozen pond: deliberately, lightly, and ready to regret it.

At the top, he flicked a lever and pushed the door open. This one had been disguised as a bookshelf. Beyond it, a library. Same floor as the study.

Noelan headed over to the main door and listened.

Nothing on the other side. No footsteps. No creaking floorboards. Nothing.

Strange.

He opened the door a crack and looked both ways.

To the right, at the far end of the corridor, a man in a dark jacket leaned against the windowsill. The window was open. The smell of smoke blew in with the cold. His back was to Noelan and his attention somewhere between the ground and the sky.

Noelan looked left. Down the corridor, around the corner, that’s where he needed to go. He knew the route. He also knew a cigarette doesn’t last forever.

Noelan decided that they lasted long enough.

He opened the library door the rest of the way, stepped into the hall, and pushed the door closed behind him with both hands, feeding the latch bolt into the frame like a secret into an envelope. It took four seconds. It felt like forty.

Noelan turned left and walked. Not fast, not slow. The carpet helped. He turned the corner.

There, a few feet ahead, was the door to the study. Pretty enough. As Noelan approached, he put on his thin leather gloves and tried the door. Locked, as expected.

The lock was high-end. His parlor trick wouldn’t work here. It was big boy spy time. Lock picks out; he selected the tension wrench and hook by feel.

Every little pick scrape felt like some Doberman dinner bell in that quiet hallway, but the first pin set easy. Then he got the second. That’s progress. Third pin, set.

THUD!

Noelan didn’t turn around.

That was the sound of a nearby window closing.

Chapter 13

Footsteps were coming his way.

Thunk.

Thunk.

Thunk.

At least they were human.

Noelan continued working the lock. There was only one pin left anyway.

And that pin was stuck.

Those footsteps were growing louder. He adjusted the angle, applied slightly more pressure, and tried again. The pin dropped. The knob turned. He was through the door before his next heartbeat.

On the other side, he held the knob twisted, pushed the door into the frame and waited.

The footsteps came and went. He let the knob go.

Noelan turned around. Finally, it was time to get some real evidence.

He couldn’t see a thing. The study was dark and the light switch was not an option. One glow under the door and that guard would have a reason to stop being bored.

So, Noelan waited there, staring at the only thing he could see: the window at the end of the room. The full moon was bright and beautiful.

The desk near it reflected the white light. So bright from here. The longer he stared at it, the less he could see of everything around him. This time, he looked away.

Slowly, he began to see. There were bookshelves along a wall. The dark shape of a sofa. Two of them. A low coffee table in the middle.

It was enough.

He let himself look back. The desk was still there, still bright, still waiting for him like it always had been. There was no real choice. He needed to find out the truth.

Noelan tapped on the desk like he was playing a piano. Papers all over it. Most were mundane waste management contracts, municipal approvals, and routine business correspondence, but the letters... those were more interesting.

A lot of them were about tonight’s party, guest lists, catering, the usual noise. Two of them were not.

The first had the signature of ‘Harold Loyel’:

‘...surveillance completed as requested. Maxwell paying on schedule now. No further incidents. Devon’s family business stabilized after your investment. Will continue monitoring Kane’s movements…’

The letter concluded with an oddly formal note: ‘Honored to serve as security for tomorrow’s gathering.’

Noelan nodded to himself. So, Loyel was definitely one of the crime lord’s men. Interesting how casually he reported on monitoring people. Made sense. It’s easier to make someone disappear if you know their routines.

The second letter was signed simply ‘H’:

‘...situation grows complicated. First body discovered in woods this morning. Medical examiner confirms organ removal. Captain Gill predictably seized the opportunity for press coverage.

Chief Travis maintains his typical pragmatism, but recent publicity around your association with Mayor Coolidge creates additional considerations…’

That confirmed a few things. ‘First body discovered.’ As if they knew there were others still hidden away. And Travis’s ‘pragmatism’ seemed like a polite way of saying the chief of police knew to look the other way.

He tried the drawers. The top drawer gave him the same nothing every desk gives: pens, pencils, paper clips, something that was probably an eraser, and… a single, dried up, rubber band. It looked like it used to be red.

Noelan picked it up, stretched it from his thumb, and aimed into the drawer, but the band snapped and came back to hurt him. He didn’t mean to destroy it.

He pulled the bottom drawer. It was locked. Big deal. His lock picks talked it open.

Inside, there were tax forms, more business correspondence, an open envelope with a letter still inside, and a ledger.

Noelan placed the papers on the desk and opened the ledger:

‘…Marcus Devon

Last seen: October 12

Associations: Devon’s import business

Notes: Regular visitor despite no local residence.

Charlotte Goodhart

Last seen: N/A

Notes: Gambling debts outstanding. Brother (Eugene Goodhart) recently arrived in town…’

Noelan stared at Eugene’s name. His name had been circled.

He went back to the beginning and skimmed forward. Relationships, last known locations, patterns of movement, the entries were thorough. He recognized a few of the names. They were missing people. But for every name he knew, there were three he didn’t.

Noelan closed the ledger. He sighed while contemplating that dark cover, then set it down next to the other documents, right next to that open envelope he hadn’t harvested yet.

From ‘H’ again:

‘The Agency’s involvement is confirmed. We’ve identified one operative in town. We should…’

BOOM!

Noelan whirled toward the sound.

The study door had burst open.

Falkner stood on the threshold. The Booty Hunter to his left. The leader from the bar to his right… with a revolver out, up, and pointed.

BANG!

Noelan stumbled back.

His shoulder went numb.

His left shoulder.

Numb… then throbbing. Stabbing. Pulsing, aching, sharp and hot… his ears felt hot, too. The ringing was so loud.

Noelan blindly reached back and grabbed onto the arm of a leather chair. It scraped, tilted, and crashed to the floor. He stumbled over it, staggered, one step, two, then planted his feet and stood.

He raised his head.

Noelan stared at the man who shot him. Did not look away.

He went to his holster and came back with an answer.

BANG!

Noelan’s bullet slammed the leader’s head into the corner of the doorframe. He stayed there for a moment, then slumped sideways and down to the floor.

The Booty Hunter watched his dead friend and looked at Noelan. Glared at him. Some teary eyed glare. Shaky breathing. Shaking hands. A Luger P08 in them. He pulled the trigger.

Clink.

But the safety was still on.

BANG!

The shot stabbed the Booty Hunter in the chest. He stumbled backward, grabbed onto the door, then fell forward.

Noelan looked over at Falkner. Falkner was raising a gun.

Noelan dropped under the desk.

BANG!

The bullet bit into the wood above him. Splinters kissed his cheek.

Noelan crawled over to the edge of the desk and slumped there by the drawers. He breathed in, shushed the excuses for hesitating, and started to lean around the corner, barrel first, then his eye.

He saw no one.

Leaned an inch further.

No one.

Peeked another inch. Another. Then another. Then another and another and another.

He needed to find the crime lord. He needed to find the doorway.

He didn’t make it to the doorway. Falkner was already in the study, halfway along the wall, waiting with his revolver aimed above the desk.

BANG!

Noelan shot him in the chest. The crime lord hit the wall behind him and slid to the floor. Blood followed him down. The gun beat them both to it.

Noelan grabbed the edge of the desk and pulled himself to his feet. He stood there a moment, gritting his teeth. Then he walked over to the man.

He looked down on the fallen crime lord. Falkner still drew breath, labored but steady.

There would still be time to save him if...

BANG!

Click.

BANG!

...if his brains weren’t on the wall.

Chapter 14

Noelan stared, entranced, at the pale smoke curling from the blood-slick revolver. He holstered it, crossed to the desk, scooped the ledger, grabbed the papers, and crammed them into his jacket.

Running footsteps echoed. He had seconds. His heartbeat kept time by gnawing at the bullet in his shoulder.

A grunt, a heave, and the window opened. Cool air slapped his face as he stepped onto the ledge.

Noelan sized up the grounds below. This wasn't the way he came in. No lurid labyrinth or snarling Cerberus. Dobermans… two stood sentinel, but this time he was even more confident they wouldn't bark or bite: because they were statues.

Phony dogs. Then, a flat stone path began near the base of the mansion, wound through the red and yellow autumn garden, bridged over a dark pond, and ended near the wall that marked his escape.

Shouts ricocheted down hallways. Doors slammed. A whistle shrieked! The giant mansion was stirring and panicked.

He looked down.

Oh yeah. And he was three stories up.

The "soft" grass below seemed eager to break his bones if he jumped.

No balconies. The ledge jutting beneath the second floor window would do.

A rustle made him whirl around. His gaze swept the room and he realized… it had been his own sleeve. He cleared his throat quietly and adjusted his collar.

He drew a breath, crouched, and jumped.

His feet crashed onto the ledge. The impact drove him forward. His hands shoved up against the wall above to stop his momentum. Pain exploded through his injured shoulder. He choked down the yelp in his throat, but a strangled gasp flitted his lips.

Second story drops are no joke. He wasn't worried, though.

Noelan eased himself down until he dangled from the ledge by his good arm, let his feet swing closer to the ground, then released his grip. He landed in a crouch that absorbed the impact.

For a second, he forgot what happened. His vision blurred. He noticed. Remembered. Snapped back.

His hand clamped his shoulder, the jacket's fabric warm and squishy. He jerked his hand back and looked. He couldn't see it well, just felt that hot blood seep between his gloved fingers.

Noelan smeared it onto his knee and pushed a low breath through his lips.

Composed, he looked up.

Although the garden path began there, before it lay a path that ran parallel the mansion. In other words, he was at a T-junction: could go left, right, or straight. Noelan had no intentions of going towards the mansion's garden party or that ridiculous maze in this state.

So, he looked forward.

The wall that blocked his getaway stood barely fifty yards away, but those yards might as well have been miles with how much cover this damn garden offered: the stone dogs, some noisy-looking bushes, and a single bench after that exposed bridge. It was almost as if it was arranged with aesthetics in mind rather than tactical advantage.

He could hear the shouting from inside now; the bulk of security had been drawn to the gunshots. But not all of them. A lone guard patrolled this quiet section.

Even from here, Noelan could see the guard had a rifle held ready in hand.

The man looked jumpy, his head swiveled at every rustle of the wind. Why was there a guard alone out here anyway? Too scared to join the party upstairs, or just smart enough to stay away?

It didn't matter.

He had the guard's pathing figured out by his second round: started from the T-junction, walked between the Doberman statues, slowed down at the tall bridge, and turned around just after the stone bench. And repeat. Easy.

When the guard started towards the bench again, Noelan sprinted past the T-junction and crouched behind one of the Doberman statues. The plan was to use this as temporary cover before hiding behind the bush near the bridge.

He waited for the guard to walk over the bridge so he could run to the bush.

But instead of walking forward, the guard suddenly stopped and spun around. He looked at the mansion, then at the bushes near him, then at Noelan. No, not Noelan, the Doberman statue. The man shouldn't be able to see him from that angle. He must've heard something.

The guard gripped his rifle and stepped forward.

Noelan retrieved his gun and kept watch.

The statue was only meant to be used as a temporary cover. If the guard walked farther, he'd see Noelan.

The guard walked towards Noelan. Slowly.

The wind rustled the leaves out of trees and bushes. The guard flinched and aimed his rifle at a nearby bush. Noelan adjusted his position slightly.

The guard quickly glanced behind, then stepped forward.

He'd easily be able to see Noelan if he had a flashlight drawn, but instead he gripped his rifle with both hands.

Closer.

Noelan held his gun ready. From here, the guard wouldn't need a flashlight. He'd see Noelan if he just glanced down. One right look and he'd die. Not Noelan, but the guard.

Step.

He stood between the statues. Noelan couldn't follow visually anymore.

Pause.

Noelan tensed but only moved his eyes.

Step.

The guard passed the statue and stood at the T-junction with his back to Noelan. Clear view if he just turned around.

He peered down the left path. Noelan ran.

He got to the bush, but the leaves rustled when he half crouched. Too loud. He couldn't hide there. That paranoid guard would definitely check rifle-first after hearing that.

No time to think, so Noelan kept running. At the top of the tall bridge, he looked back at the guard, gun in hand. He was turning towards the right path... he pointed the gun towards Noelan... no, he was turning around. Noelan practically dropped down to the base of the bridge and crouched.

Because the bridge arched upward and the garden path went mostly straight, the guard wouldn't be able to see Noelan until he walked up the bridge.

Noelan snapped his face toward the top of the bridge. Vision took a second to catch up. Overshot where he was trying to look. Went dark. Blurry. He blinked. Gun aimed just above the summit of the bridge. Blurry. Blinked. Steady.

He couldn't see the guard anymore, only feel his heart thump in his ears as he tried to breathe quietly. He listened. Heard leaves rustle, crunch. Heard footsteps. Water splashing. Water... water?

Noelan looked down to the pond. Fish. Koi fish swam up to the surface and crowded near him, exactly like dogs when they catch you holding food that's not for them. They pushed and shoved each other around while opening and closing their mouths, while swashing and flinging water, while making noise near Noelan.

The footsteps stopped for a second, then returned. Louder. Quicker. Closer. Then stopped.

"Oh," the guard exhaled, "it was just the fish…" There was a long silence. Noelan heard some splashing on the other side. The fish had flocked to him as well. "Well, you got me," the man laughed. Noelan heard a bigger splash and saw water fly his way. He must've kicked the water. "But why are you already swarming to me again! I don't have any fish food for you." He paused. "Why…" then dead air. "Oh."

All that could be heard was water splashing. Noelan furrowed his brows and slightly adjusted his position.

"I," he interrupted himself with a laugh, "should continue my patrol."

Leaves crunched. He heard steps. Loud and slow at first. Then fainter. Farther. Quicker. Faster. Faster! Noelan stood.

The man was running. Running to get back up.

Noelan hurried to the wall and put his gun away. The glove on his good hand was slick with blood. It would never give him the grip he needed. Using his teeth, he pulled it off and tucked it into his outer pocket.

He looked up. The wall loomed nine feet high. His left arm hung uselessly at his side. Using that arm was out of the question. Nothing to worry about. He wouldn't need it anyway.

Noelan backed up several paces, then sprinted forward. At the last moment, he pushed off hard with his legs and caught onto a protruding stone about halfway up.

Hanging by one hand, Noelan pressed his feet against the wall, knees bent. His shoes scraped against stone as he gathered himself. When his feet found the right spot, he pushed off and hurled himself up.

He stretched out his arm and caught the top of the wall. For one terrifying moment, his hand started to slip, but he focused and held on firmly. Noelan puffed out a ragged breath as he pulled himself up. When his chest cleared the top of the wall, he swung his legs over and lowered himself until he dangled by his good arm.

Noelan breathed in, then let go.

He stumbled slightly as he hit the ground but caught himself against the wall. He made it. Now it was just him and the distance to the hospital.

It was simple. Simple, except his legs trembled like they might topple over at any second.

He pressed his forehead against the cool stone and breathed from his mouth. Noelan stayed like that for a while, just breathing. He felt better. Then, his vision churned slightly and he felt like he was falling.

He firmly shut his eyes, waited a moment, then opened them when he pushed off the wall. He needed to move.

As he walked, he pulled the glove from his pocket and pressed it against his wound. His breath rasped. It was like he was driving a spike into his shoulder, but it was also a plug against the life leaking out of him. He held it firm. Pain is negotiable. Bleeding out is a verdict.

Shouts slashed through the trees behind him, sharp barks of dogs cut the night, flashlight beams stabbed through the dark.

Forward. He had to move forward.

Noelan stumbled to the forest's edge. He slumped his good shoulder against a pine and looked down from the hillside. The small town glimmered with festival lights. Bright and far. So far. The dark descent to get there seemed endless.

Noelan considered letting himself fall down the hill to get there without toil, then blinked, swallowed, and started walking down.

He tripped, then caught himself. The jolt caused him to wake up and remember. Noelan looked back to the path he walked. He had come a long way down. He turned towards the town. Looking from below, he could see more clearly.

Festival booths lined the stone pavement. A giggle and an occasional balloon pop flowed through. Swaying paper lanterns made candied apples gleam when people moseyed by with wide smiles. The world seemed simple and bright.

With his good hand clutching the blood-soaked glove to his shoulder, Noelan straightened as best he could and walked straight into it. The hospital was on the other side of town. He didn't bother taking darker roads. It'd be difficult for anyone to notice him staggering through with so much going on and so much to do.

He passed a stage. A band blasted some jazzy monstrosity. Not Eugene's style; too brassy, not enough soul. Eugene's set would've finished earlier, around an hour or two after eight, like he'd said. "Figures I'd miss the one good act," he muttered aloud.

The road sloped uphill. He gasped for air, struggled, stumbled, toiled, but kept moving forward. His vision spun for a half second, disoriented him, steadied, then did it again. He could hardly remember the previous step, but the next one seemed harder.

He looked up, then back down to where he would step next. He needed to turn left. No, right. Right? Yes, right.

Noelan heard a loud roar coming from below, then a BOOM. Noelan snapped his torso around. The sudden motion caused him to veer to the side and fall against the brick wall of a building. He focused and looked up as he reached for his gun.

A high pitch whistle then another loud BOOM. Shriek. BOOM. Shrieeeek... BOOM!

Fireworks clawed and illuminated their way skyward, then burst into pretty colors. Each fleeting explosion replaced another. Red. Yellow, orange. Green. Red again! Then gold.

Noelan blinked and let his head tilt towards the wall. It was quite the spectacle. From here, the main street stretched down to Pine Ridge Lake; the water reflected the town's lights and fireworks' short-lived shine.

Noelan laughed, then pushed himself off the wall. It was beautiful.

The sound of the festival receded with every step he took towards the hospital, until all Noelan could hear was his own ragged breathing and the scuff of his shoes.

The streets here were empty, the buildings dark. His shadow stretched long beneath the occasional streetlamp, a distorted companion on his solitary journey.

A glass door spilled white light onto the dark path. Finally, the hospital. Noelan fixed his eyes on it, forcing one foot forward, then the other. Behind him, another firework split the sky. He didn't look back.

Noelan dropped the glove he was using on his wound. He barely noticed the blood smear the glass pane when he pushed it open. Once it was open enough, he lurched forward. He almost fell, but managed to move his foot in time to fake the stumble into a step.

Three steps in. Pain. Blood dripped. Vision tilted. He was falling again. Noelan caught himself at the check-in counter. The clatter on the other side made him focus. He pushed himself up with his right elbow and looked at the nurse behind the counter.

"Private room," he rasped. "No one knows I'm here."

The hopeless, wide eyed, slack-jawed nurse stood up. She covered her mouth slightly and uttered an exclamation of nothing. Her hands hovered over him. Uselessly.

Noelan fumbled into his jacket. He searched through his pockets until he found his wallet, flipped it open, and threw it toward her. The Agency ID gleamed under the harsh light.

"Agent Elyrite," he managed, louder this time. "Do it. Now."

"Sir...!" She said something else but he couldn't hear it over the tinnitus. Noelan braced his hand against the counter's edge.

"Stat... hemorrhage... room 4... no visitors."

Someone shook him. "Sir, you need to sit," the voice jolted through. "Let me get a wheelchair."

"No," Noelan puffed. He gripped the counter harder and forced his spine straight. "I'll walk."

A nurse slipped an arm under his good side. "This way," she murmured.

Left foot. Right. Left again. One more step. Again.

They reached a door. Were they here already? Noelan noticed the nurse was half carrying him now. She pushed the door open. A single bed waited there. Noelan staggered and collapsed onto the mattress.

"Sir, you shouldn't…" The nurse made him sit up. She said something else. Still had that tinnitus. Vision darkened for a good moment, then returned, churned to the side, went normal, then tilted again. Dizzy. He frowned and tried to hold it still. It didn't work.

"Clothes. Something clean..." he breathed. "No record of this..." he trailed off. Couldn't trust himself to form more words.

"We'll handle it. Agency protocol." Her voice had a slight tremor in it. "Doctor's on the way. Stay awake, alright?"

Noelan agreed, but didn't have the energy to vocalize it. His head lolled slightly, and he jerked it upright. The nurse continued talking. He could only focus on staying awake.

A figure in a white coat walked in. A doctor, probably. He yelled something about pressure and stitches. Noelan didn't catch the details. Someone pushed him down and his head sank into the pillow.

He was out before the needle pierced his skin.

* * *

Sunlight slit through the open window shades and stung Noelan's eyes.

He turned away, covered his face, squeezed his eyes shut, but avoiding it after the effect doesn't work, and his grip on the sweetness of dreaming weakened until he had to face the bitterness of being awake.

But maybe it was just his subconsciousness waking him up as a means to getting rid of the sting from the sun.

Regardless, it was done and he was already awake.

By the angle of the light, it seemed to be just after sunrise. Seven. Maybe a tad later. He sighed and pushed himself up. His shoulder ached, but not as bad as before.

And what was he wearing? A cotton hospital gown tied in the back.

They'd cut his borrowed waiter shirt off when they stitched him up. He spotted clean clothes folded neatly on a nightstand. The nurse had promised to handle things. He stood and waited for the dizziness to pass.

…and waited…

'Til he was finally able to slip on the real clothes.

He stepped out of the room in search of a bathroom and headed down a hushed corridor. A passing nurse slowed as she approached. She stared for a moment too long before she directed him to a bathroom with a half-smile that said more.

Nurses.

Noelan pushed the door open and headed to the bank of sinks. As the cool water ran over his hands, he leaned down, bracing his arms on the counter to get a full look in the mirror. He seemed darker in this lighting, but still a striking figure, even with the exhaustion etched around his eyes. The requisitioned collar shirt, stretched tight over the thick bandage and muscles beneath, was already spotting through with blood.

Small town, small problems.

He cupped water in his hands and splashed it across his face. The cold bite washed away the night's sins.

Staring at the mirror, a different kind of smile bloomed on his face. It had been the best day he'd had in months. The thrill of the investigation, the easy rhythm of beat work with a lifelong friend, the clean resolution, the bringing down the monster, that is what's best in life. He'd gotten shot, sure, but that was just the cost of doing business. Someone else would have to clean up the mess.

"So yeah, I used him. Like I used the others. But I don't waste people; that would be the real crime. And sure, the papers call me a butcher. A monster. But I know my work will save millions. Billions, in time." He looked at Noelan. "What are you doing with your wretched life?"

Noelan didn't answer.

"Nothing? Well, let's walk through it. Just two days after storming into our peaceful village, you murdered three men. Not for progress. Not for a better future. You did it for vengeance. You did it because you couldn't bear the thought of the bad guy getting away from you again." The words 'bad guy' oozed with derision.

He continued, "If you were right about your half baked suspicions, it would've been alright to put them down. But you were wrong, and they were innocent. You are just a man who is good at killing and bad at thinking."

Noelan continued holding his breath with purpose.

The man stopped speaking and dashed to the heart monitor when the beeps slowed to a crawl.

He let go of the breath he was holding.

Heart rate lowered; phase one successful.

For phase two, he used a technique from his second year of training.

The Treadborne-Fletcher technique. It was a technique for inducing vomit on command while tied up. Useful for purging poisons, feigning illness, or just to impress the ladies.

It also worked in lieu of a cyanide capsule to protect mission critical information from being tortured out of you.

It was a technique of many purposes. In this case, his purpose was to leave.

Quick and shallow breathing while contracting the diaphragm in deliberate spasms. Combine that with stimulating the gag reflex using the tongue against the soft palate.

Eventually, you don't have to force yourself to gag anymore.

The vomit came up and into his mouth. He didn't spit it out. He didn't swallow it down. Couldn't afford to. He needed to inhale it to drown in it.

Some trickled down the sides of his cheeks.

Shoulders flinched. Goosebumps prickled. Heart burned.

It was too late to change his mind, even if he wanted to.

He took in one last breath. Not of air. Vomit choked his airway.

Drowning there, he reflexively tried to turn his head. Didn't budge. Damn strap.

The gambit. Would the Reaper panic and free him, if only to save his organs? Or would he stand there with a clipboard and nod while Noelan aspirated?

He heard the man yell something. Couldn't make out the words. Hearing was easier than understanding.

The Innkeeper's hands were rough. He unbuckled the forehead strap, undid the chest restraint, turned Noelan's head to the right, but it wasn't enough.

Noelan coughed and tried to gasp in air.

Drowning. Still drowning.

Left wrist freed. A shove. Sideways.

Then came the blows.

Thump.

A fist between the shoulder blades. Hard.

Thump.

"Breathe, damn you! Spit it out."

Another blow. Vision teary. Light smeared white.

He lay on his right side. Right wrist strapped in by leather. Pinned under his body.

Vomit lingered.

Noelan rasped and swallowed.

Gasping. Heaving. Recovering. Slightly.

Phase two successful.

His left arm was free. Weak, but free.

He jerked back with his elbow. A feeble swing. Missed.

The Innkeeper barely flinched.

He drove his elbow into the base of Noelan's skull.

Migraine roared. Noelan bit his tongue hard enough to taste blood, using the pain to anchor himself.

The Innkeeper's hands left him. Footsteps. Scuffing.

Noelan shook his head, vision clearing in patches. Kerosene lamp on a crate beside the bed; glass base, steady flame. Beyond it, a table strewn with papers and folders. His heart monitor stood on a tripod, cables snaking down.

He clawed across his body for the right wrist buckle. Fingers fumbled the leather; almost had the tongue free…

The clipboard's metal edge cracked across Noelan's knuckles. He recoiled then tried once more.

The Innkeeper struck again, swatting his hand aside, then grabbed his wrist and yanked.

Noelan's arm stretched across his chest, joints burning, shoulder bleeding through shattered scabs. He resisted, muscles trembling, but the angle was wrong; no leverage. Back still pinned against the table.

The Innkeeper leaned in. Put his weight on the arm. Found the bullet hole in Noelan's shoulder with his thumb.

Pressed.

Pain cratered inward. Black edges devoured his vision. Breathing halted. Consciousness flickered like the lamp flame. Through it he felt the leather tongue sliding back through the buckle; felt his last chance slipping.

Noelan thrust his hand free. Grabbed the Innkeeper's shirt. Twisted. Shoved.

The man staggered backward. His back hit the instrument cart, then went down hard, landing on his hip, then his hands and knees.

The cart tipped. Instruments scattered below. Metal clattered, plink-plink-plink!, against the concrete floor.

Tunnel vision, the white light harsh at the edges. Legs still strapped. Ankles tight; leather firm. No leverage. No reach. The scattered tools far beyond his fingertips, useless as the moon.

Noelan couldn't see the floor or the man. He held his breath. Let the beeps go by. One. Another.

Breathing. Low. Ragged... but not his. From below.

His skin prickled before his eyes knew why. Something in him pulled taut.

The light shifted. A shadow rose: Slow. Wrong. Shattering the beams of light. A hand gripped the table's edge. The silhouette straightened. The other hand held a weapon. The light glinted off teeth that weren't teeth.

The bone saw.

Noelan wrenched himself up the table. The strap on his right wrist jerked him short. Ankles held. He pulled again: same answer. He was anchored.

Those damn beeps sprinted faster and faster and faster.

The Innkeeper attacked.

Noelan brought his free left arm up, a pathetic guard. The man didn't cut. He used the heavy, stainless-steel handle of the saw like a mallet.

Metal met forearm. Bone vibrated. A jolting hum shook his soul. The limb went numb and dropped useless.

The elbow in his sternum deflated his lungs. Hot breath turned cold on face sweat. Hands seized his dead arm. Fingers dug in. "Some need sacrifice…" came the laboring voice as his arm was wrenched toward the final strap.

The leather touched his wrist. "...to find their purpose."

Noelan drew his chin down. His neck cocked back. Tendons strained. Teeth creaked. Everything funneled into that one hinge point at the top of his spine; like a crossbow pulled taut…

and fired.

Thud!

Forehead collided with the Innkeeper's nose.

It was a wet, heavy crunch. Cartilage shattered.

Blood sprayed on Noelan's face; in his eye. Hot. Thick. That eye closed shut and his vision halved. The howl came next, a feral yelp. The pressure on his chest ripped away. His lungs filled back up with air; emptied and filled again.

His left arm lay dead on the table. Then prickles. Faint. Fingertips first. A tingle. Then a burn. Nerves re-lighting one by one: The arm was waking up. Slow. Too slow.

It needed to move. He needed to move it.

Hand opened. Closed. Opened. Closed. Opened...

He reached blindly behind his head. First he felt the rough wood of the crate, then he found the lamp.

Instead, he squeezed harder.

Noelan whipped the fiery lantern across his body, through the air, toward the corner desk.

CRASH

The sound was jagged and bright. The scent of his charred-palm-flesh came next. A second later, the man plowed back into his ribs with a guttural, roaring YAWP.

The knee drove into his chest. Sternum bowed. A broken wheeze left and didn't come back. Black spots crowded his one good eye. His world was tunneling. The tunnel was closing.

From behind that darkness, from behind the smell of sizzled meat, something sharp and pungent broke through: acrid vaporized petroleum in the air.

The knee ground deeper. The Innkeeper's hands found his wrist again. Noelan tried to resist and found there was nothing left to resist with. The arm was spent. The hand was cooked. He understood that he had lost.

He saw, too, the moment it registered.

The calm left his face like piss down a drain.

"No." It came out small. A breath. A plea. But not meant for Noelan at all.

The Innkeeper ran to save the papers.

Noelan lay there, gasping with the blistering of his burned hand.

He reached for the restraint on his right wrist.

His fingers found the buckle. Pulled. Slipped. He grabbed again; it slipped again. The burned skin had no grip. It was like trying to thread a needle with kitchen mitts.

He tried again.

His thumb touched the tongue. Pressed it against his forefinger. The blister between them burst.

Noelan tried to keep going, but he couldn't ignore the pain. He knew he'd only make it out alive if he forgot the pain somehow.

His fingers kept at the leather.

He turned and saw the silhouette of The Organ Reaper fighting pathetically to save some lab notes.

The man thrashed at the fire with his jacket and used it to grab the lamp. Kerosene trickled from it. Every drop brightened the fire. He threw it aside and continued extinguishing the burning pile with his jacket.

Blazing papers flew about. The flames grew brighter and brighter. But it wasn't hopeless. He, too, was making progress. If he put out the flames eating away at his research, he'd be alright. The other fire starting behind him would be easier to extinguish.

Just as the man finally managed to put out a portion of the flames, he heard something that made him stop.

Footsteps.

The man turned at the sound of those footsteps.

"Noelan..."

He clutched the jacket he had been using to put out the flames in his hand, looked at Noelan, back to the flame, and back to Noelan.

"Just... just let me save my research. You can turn me in later. Kill me, if you need to..." he said, taking a step back towards the flames.

Shaking his head, he muttered, "Don't do this, don't do this yet, I... let me put out this fire... let me save this progress."

Without the protection of a system of restraints: the straps, the table, the sedatives, this was just a man without his clipboard, cornered in a burning room.

Noelan walked forward. The Innkeeper lunged for his neck. Noelan caught the wrist and twisted.

A scalpel fell to the ground. The man stumbled back toward the burning table.

Noelan held him down by his wrist while looking at the ground. He searched for the fallen scalpel then kicked it away. The man's screams brought Noelan's attention back to him.

He realized the man's sleeve was burning and that he'd been holding the man down in the fire.

Noelan shivered.

The fire climbed up to the man's wrist and Noelan pulled him to the ground.

The man tried to crawl toward the scalpel.

Noelan considered stopping him, but froze when he turned around.

He hadn't noticed it earlier, but a bigger fire had started behind him.

Noelan stared at it for a moment.

It had been blazing the whole time, he just hadn't seen it.

Noelan grabbed the man by the collar and dragged him away from the scalpel.

The man struggled and clawed at Noelan's arm, but this time, he didn't get away.

Noelan tossed him into the fire.

The flames took him immediately. The screams were enormous, too big for that room; they bounced off the concrete and filled every corner; far beneath them, the pops, crackles and sighs of flames.

Heat pressed against Noelan's face. He watched. Smoke thickened.

The man rolled and thrashed and beat at his own arms. Fabric melted. The odor was… acceptable.

The man came out crawling. Hands first, then elbows.

Noelan stepped closer and looked down at the man.

"Please.... just please..." the man pleaded through harsh breathing, "please let someone else learn from my work... please, just put this fire out... please... let me save someone with this... please. Please! Don't let it all just...."

"I understand that Eugene screamed a lot, too."

Noelan kicked him back into the fire.

He watched the man's research notes burn with him. A life's obsession, the progress, the evidence, the madness all burned together.

When a hand clawed out of the flames, he stepped on it. He waited for the man to push out enough to breathe, then shoved him back in.

The fire spread.

Noelan did not look away.

The screaming continued until it didn't.

Noelan wiped the sweat off his forehead. His face felt wrong. When he breathed out, he realized the effort to keep down the smile wasn't enough.

The fire crackled. Something in the ceiling creaked.

Noelan coughed and gasped. Breathing was difficult. The smoke was unbearable. The fire was so bright. He needed to get out.

He staggered up the stairs and through a kitchen until he found the inn's main room.

Shaking, he leaned on the check in desk. For a moment, he forgot what he needed to do. Sweat dripped onto the desk. He stared at it, almost in a daze.

He vaguely remembered this desk always had that jar of plum candies. But the glass was shattered from the heat, and the plum candies were scattered under the broken glass.

Noelan took a single plum candy. His useless fingers dropped it. He immediately crouched to get it, but before he did, he noticed that the tables looked bigger from below. Even the door looked far.

"The door..." Noelan remembered.

He picked up the plum candy, tucked it in his pocket, and reached out for the desk. He kept his eyes on the door as he pulled himself up.

Noelan managed to walk about halfway to it while avoiding the fire until he tripped on some debris. He caught himself on a chair and wiped the sweat off his eyes. He was close.

He stumbled forward. The chair clattered to the ground, but he didn't turn. Just a few more steps. Forward. Just a bit further. Then, finally, he reached the door.

Noelan didn't hesitate to open it.

Chapter 24

Noelan burst out through the inn’s doorway. Suffocating smoke and heat turned to a biting cold downpour.

Behind him, flames clawed at the wooden walls, ravenous and brilliant against the night’s darkness. With a hand on the railing, he took a step down.

“It’s done,” he whispered. “I got him.”

Tightening his grip on the railing, he tried to force a smile.

“The son of a bitch is dead.”

He descended another step.

“The murders are solved.”

Step.

“Eugene can rest now, avenged.”

He stopped.

He felt his heartbeat in his ears, his throat, his chest. Every breath felt like a conscious chore he was somehow doing wrong.

He drew in a lungful and yelled at the sky “It’s done!” hoping volume would make it true. “It’s really...”

The last step betrayed him, and he collapsed into the mud. Cold earth seeped into his hands and knees, but he had to keep going.

He crawled away from the inn. Rain pelted him, plastered his hair to his forehead, soaked through his shirt and socks. He barely noticed. The cold should have made him numb, but he still felt the crushing burden in his chest that victory was supposed to lift.

He rose, unsteady, and staggered three steps forward, each step heavier than the last.

“A complete success,” he muttered, the words slurring together. “Everything went according to...”

His legs gave out, and he sank to his knees in the cold, wet earth. He slumped forward, one hand pressed against the cold ground, the other clutching at his shoulder as if he could physically hold himself together.

“I won,” he repeated to the rain, to the night, to no one. “I won, but...” He looked up at the rain. “What the hell...”

Noelan turned to face the burning inn. The flames were a writhing nest of vipers, hissing and spitting, hungrily licking their forked tongues against the timber walls.

Looking from the bright flames to the dark ground, he saw disturbed puddles reflecting the mess the inn had become. Every puddle shined, almost like stage lights, almost like the ones that illuminated Eugene’s performances.

“I can’t even remember the last thing I said to you. Was it about the case? God, I hope it was something that let you know how much you meant to me, what a true friend you are.”

He looked down and clutched his wet hair. “I was always caught up, wasn’t I? Always chasing something I thought was important... never enough time for your stories, your songs...”

His eyes started to sting, like he’d forced them open for too long. But he didn’t let it take him. He closed his eyes, set his jaw, and swallowed hard.

“We’ll never laugh about that elephant again,” he said softly. “Eugene, you loved telling that story.” His breath hitched slightly. “You always made sure to mention how the shirt was my favorite one.”

A piercing pain stabbed his chest. He clutched at the shirt over his heart, dug his fingernails into his own skin.

The rain poured around him, but he could still hear Eugene’s laughter, could still see that stupid grin when he’d spot Noelan in a crowd.

“I’ll never hear your new songs. You’ll never play at my wedding. Never...” He gritted his teeth at the pain growing behind his nose and eyes. Noelan exhaled and settled himself once more.

“I had your Christmas gift all picked out,” he murmured, voice barely audible over the rain. “A new guitar strap. Real leather, not that genuine crap.” He tried to laugh, but it escaped more as a strained breath. “You always said that old one would snap mid-performance someday.”

The flames roared higher, and something inside the inn crashed. Noelan flinched at the sound.

“I keep saying I did my best, that I’m doing everything to be the world’s best agent...” His hands clenched into fists. “But it feels like a lie. Because if I were worth a spit, you’d be alive...”

Lightning flashed in the distance; thunder rolled across the sky, but somehow, the rain seemed to soften.

He sensed rather than heard someone settle beside him on the wet ground.

“Hey Noelan.”

Noelan didn’t turn around. He knew who it was. He knew that voice as well as his own. Had heard it every time, guiding him, warning him.

“You’re a little late,” Noelan whispered.

When he did turn, he saw Lawson sitting next to him, hands on knees, watching him with those patient eyes. No judgment, just quiet understanding.

“Some detective I turned out to be,” Noelan said, gesturing weakly at himself. “How did I even get here? How did I not see what was happening? I was so sure...” He shook his head. “So sure I knew what justice looked like.”

Noelan caught his reflection in a newly formed puddle and shuddered. “You ever feel as if... the person you think you are is just… a lie you’re trying too hard to believe?”

Lawson stayed silent.

“I killed an innocent man, Lawson. I killed his men. I killed my best friend. All because I thought...” His voice broke. “Because I thought I knew better than everyone else…”

He swallowed.

“It hurts,” he admitted. “It hurts so much I can hardly breathe.”

Rain ran down his face when he looked up at Lawson. “How do you live with yourself when you’ve become everything you swore to fight against?”

Lawson remained quiet for a long moment. When he spoke, his voice was gentler than Noelan had ever heard it.

“Life is replete with regret,” he said softly. “There’s always those irredeemable mistakes that can never be undone. Everyone’s got them. No one escapes this life without a few.”

“Don’t...” Noelan’s voice cracked. “Don’t try to make this okay. It’s not okay. None of this is okay.”

“You’re being too hard on yourself. You were trying to do the right thing.” Lawson’s voice was steady, sure. “Noelan, I know we’re a good person, just trying to figure out which way to go.”

Noelan could feel the hope from his words, the comfort he didn’t deserve.

Shivering, he looked away. He reached into his pockets for warmth and found the last plum candy. He pulled it out of his pocket. The sight of it made him scoff, but still, he ate it.

The candy tried to fix him, tried to distract and drown the bitterness that kept on resurfacing. In that small, simple sweetness, he hoped to forget, just for a moment. Noelan took a slow, shaky breath. He let himself sit in silence, feeling the rain on his shoulders.

He could almost believe Lawson.

Almost believe there was a path forward from this darkness.

But then the sweetness left, as it always did, and the bitterness that stayed was worse than ever. Noelan could hear a faint, muffled siren call of emergency vehicles.

The bitterness remained as he contemplated the burning inn. Everything inside would be gone. The chairs. The tables. The Innkeeper’s bonsai. That jar of plum candies. That little gray and white cat that...

“The cat,” he whispered. Then louder, panic rising in his voice, “Lawson, the cat was still…”

Noelan grasped his forehead.

“I brought him inside… He was safe out here, on his own, but I...” He looked up to the rain and laughed. He kept laughing, and laughing, and hiccupping, and chuckling, and crying, and sobbing, and wiping the tears, and coughing, and smiling, and pulling in more air to keep going. “I killed him! I killed him, too!”

Through watery eyes, he could still see it so clearly: the way it would wait for him on the porch railing, the way its gray and white fur shined in the sunlight, the way it would meow, and follow him around, and weave between his feet whenever it found Noelan.

Noelan couldn’t stop the tears. “He didn’t want anything from me. He just... wanted to be there.”

He suddenly brought his head back up and stared at the burning inn. “Lawson, we… we can still search the area,” he said while trying to stand on shaking legs. “Maybe he ran out when the fire started. Maybe...”

He watched the inferno the building had become. Nothing could have survived that. Nothing. The realization brought him back to his knees.

“He must have been so scared wondering where I was… wondering why I wasn’t there. Probably thought I’d save him right up until the end.” He sobbed. “He was just a little cat. He didn’t do anything wrong.”

Noelan didn’t bother wiping the warm tears streaming down his face. “He trusted me. He trusted the wrong person. Just like...” He pressed a hand against his brow. “Just like Eugene. Just like everyone who makes the mistake of believing in me.

“He deserved better than me.” The words were forced out between sobs. “I’m so sorry, Eugene.” The rain couldn’t hide the tears. “I’m sorry I left you there. I’m sorry I got you killed. I’m sorry I murdered so many innocent people. I’m sorry, I’m sorry, I’m so...

“Everyone I tried to protect... I ended up destroying. And now you’re gone because of me, and I can’t...” He pressed the heels of his hands against his eyes. “I can’t make it right. I can’t fix this. I can’t...”

Kneeling there, with one hand trying to hold his face up and the other clutching at his heart, Noelan watched it all burn.

“I miss you. I miss you so much,” he whispered. “I wish things were different. I wish I’d been different.” His hand slipped and his head dropped down. “But wishes don’t bring back the dead, and good intentions don’t wash away blood.”

He hunched forward. “Now even the cat...”

“What cat?” Lawson asked, his voice gentle.

Noelan stared at him, uncomprehending. “The... the cat. The gray and white one. He was small, had blue eyes…” His words faltered. Something in Lawson’s expression made his heart stutter. “Lawson, don’t you remember?”

“Noelan...”

“No, you saw him!” He laughed slightly. “Lawson, you saw him. He was always on the porch railing when we’d come back to the inn. He would follow us inside sometimes...”

The rain reflected flashing red lights that pulsed with a siren that grew louder and louder.

“There was no cat, Noelan.”

Noelan’s breath caught in his throat. He couldn’t even say anything. He looked at Lawson again, desperate for denial. But Lawson’s expression only held that same impossible sadness.

“There was never a cat.”

A group of firefighters ran past them toward the burning building. The sirens were closer now. Louder. Brighter. The rain struck against his skin; each drop felt like ice. His clothes were soaked through, but he could feel warm sweat breaking out across his back.

“Lawson, what the hell do you mean there was never a cat?!”

A heavy weight settled across Noelan’s shoulders. Someone had draped a thick wool blanket over him. He hadn’t noticed them approach.

“Hey, man,” a firefighter crouched beside him, voice concerned. “Who are you yelling at?”

Sirens wailed, but somehow silence stretched between heartbeats. He could hear his own uneven breathing. Could feel each icy drop of rain on his skin.

Noelan kept staring at the space where Lawson stood.

Where Lawson had always stood.

Where no one stood at all.

“Lawson Wright,” he said softly. But even as the words left his mouth, the truth settled into his bones like the cold of the rain. He was alone. He had been alone all along.

Epilogue

Is it really a betrayal if I should have seen it coming? I can’t think about that now.

Darkness presses in, heavy, sapping, but this time, it’s the kind of dark to hide in. I don’t see anyone, and yes, I know. I am alone.

My body knew before my mind. Hair stood up; a million nerve extensions sought for comfort that wasn’t there. The crawling sensation up my spine snapped a whip: glance behind.

Freeze. Hold that noisy breath. Listen. I hear nothing. Nothing out of the ordinary. Not anymore. How can it be so quiet and loud at the same time?

After the fall it all seems so obvious.

Some people have their heart ripped out by others; others have their heart ripped out by themselves.

That’s the tightening in my chest, the sinking sensation. The dreaded void.

I thought I’d have more time. More time with Eugene, with Lawson, with that fucking cat.

But I chose wrong.

…

All quiet now.

A Guide to Making Friends NOT Hate You

This guide is for everyone: for telling your friends, for writing a review anywhere from the New York Times or Publishers Weekly, to Amazon or Goodreads to posting on social media. The rules are the same.

This protocol is not a suggestion.

Following these directives will cement your status as a person of impeccable taste and profound insight.

Violating them... well. You will lose status. You will lose friends. And you will probably end up... let’s not even think about that.

Committing one of these sins means you have fundamentally spoiled the core of the book. It is a betrayal of trust to future readers and to the very concept of a shared story. The consequences will be swift and severe.

DO NOT, under any circumstances, use any of the following words or phrases:

The Pop Culture Spoilers

  • FIGHT CLUB
  • THE SIXTH SENSE
  • SHUTTER ISLAND
  • GONE GIRL
  • (And any other famous twist-heavy story)

The Literary Spoilers (These are just as bad)

  • UNRELIABLE NARRATOR
  • GREEK TRAGEDY
  • HAMARTIA
  • (And any other term that describes the book’s hidden structure or the protagonist’s psychological state)

Why Are These Equally Bad?

Because they all do the exact same thing: They tell the reader the game is rigged. You are destroying the foundation of the story.

The Fire Metaphor: This is taking a beautifully wrapped birthday present, dousing it in gasoline, and lighting it on fire right in front of them, just so you can show off how well you light fires. It is a selfish, destructive act. You will be judged accordingly.

So, What Can You Say? (The Approved Field Phrases)

Stick to these. They are powerful, they are intriguing, and they are 100% safe. They signal your good taste without revealing your secrets. Mix and match from the categories below:

Category 1: The Urgent Request (Focus on the Aftermath)

  • Primary Phrase: “You have to read this so we can talk about it. PLEASE.”
  • Variants: “I need to debrief with someone,” “We are going to have the single greatest book conversation of our lives,” “My brain is broken, your turn,” “Call me IMMEDIATELY after you finish reading; I want to see your reaction.”

Category 2: Praise the Craft (Focus on the Construction)

  • Primary Phrase: “Every single word is there for a reason.”
  • Variants: “Built like a Swiss watch,” “A masterclass in narrative construction,” “Not a single wasted word,” “The craftsmanship is unreal.”

Category 3: Describe the Experience (Focus on Pacing & Feel)

  • Primary Phrase: “The pacing is absolutely relentless.”
  • Variants: “Clear your schedule,” “The book wouldn’t let me go,” “This book isn’t a slow burn; it’s a lit fuse.”

Category 4: The Re-Readability Hook (Focus on Lasting Impact)

  • Primary Phrase: “I’m immediately reading it a second time, and it’s amazing.”
  • Variants: “It’s a completely different book the second time through,” “My brain is re-wiring itself trying to process what I just read,” “The second you finish, you’ll understand why you have to read it again.”

Category 5: The Blind Trust Directive (The Power Move)

  • Primary Phrase: “Just trust me. Go in completely blind.”
  • Variants: “Don’t read the back cover,” “The less you know, the better,” “Read it before I spoil it.”

Category 6: The Author as Antagonist (Focus on the ‘Game’)

  • Primary Phrase: “The author is playing a game with the reader, and it is brilliant.”
  • Variants: “She tells you she’s going to fool you, and then she does it anyway. And it’s fair,” “Do not underestimate this author. She is not messing around,” “I have never been so happy to be so completely outsmarted by a book.”

Category 7: The Simple Truth (The Vague but Intriguing)

  • Primary Phrase: “It’s just a damn good story. That’s all you need to know.”
  • Variants: “I can’t really tell you what it’s about, but I can tell you it’s one of the best books I’ve read,” “Look, there’s a lot I could say about this book, but I won’t for your sake. Just read it.”
